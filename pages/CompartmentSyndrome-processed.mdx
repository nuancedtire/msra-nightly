# Compartment Syndrome

Compartment syndrome is a serious condition caused by raised tissue pressure within a closed osteofascial space, which can develop after a long bone fracture, hemorrhage, or direct soft-tissue injury. This increased pressure eventually restricts capillary blood flow in the compartment, leading to potential tissue necrosis. Early recognition and intervention are crucial to prevent permanent damage.

## **Diagnosis**

Diagnosing compartment syndrome relies heavily on clinical judgment and requires a high index of suspicion to ensure swift recognition. It is classically associated with the **6 Ps**:

- **P**ain
- **P**oikilothermia
- **P**araesthesia
- **P**aralysis
- **P**ulsesness
- **P**allor

The last three signs are uncommon and appear late in progression. The most typical clinical features are:

- **Pain out of proportion** to examination findings, worsened by passive movement.
- A **feeling of pressure** within the compartment.

Common sites include the leg (especially anterior and deep posterior compartments) and the volar forearm compartment. These are typically involved following tibial shaft and supracondylar fractures respectively.

## **Investigations**

When the diagnosis is uncertain, intracompartmental pressure measurements are warranted. Additionally, serum creatine kinase and urine myoglobin should be assessed as markers of muscle necrosis.

## **Management**

**Compartment syndrome is a surgical emergency** that demands prompt action:

- **Dressing release**: Casts and/or dressings should be removed immediately to reduce pressure.
- **Fasciotomy**: This is the first-line treatment in patients without external dressings.
- **Supportive care**: Includes administration of analgesics (e.g., morphine) and IV fluids.
- **Debridement and amputation**: Reserved for cases where necrosis of muscle groups is present, indicated by a failure of muscles to contract in response to electrocoagulation.

Post-fasciotomy management should emphasize meticulous wound care, using skin grafts for coverage, and initiating physical and occupational therapy. It is also critical to treat the underlying cause, e.g., fracture.

<details>
<summary>Common Signs and Symptoms</summary>

- **Chest Pain**
  - Crushing or pressure-like
  - May radiate to left arm, jaw, or back
  - Often accompanied by sweating

- **Respiratory Symptoms**
  - Shortness of breath
  - Rapid breathing
  - Orthopnea

- **Other Symptoms**
  - Nausea and vomiting
  - Fatigue
  - Light-headedness
  - Anxiety

</details>
