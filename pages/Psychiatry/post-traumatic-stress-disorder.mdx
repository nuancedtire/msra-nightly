# Understanding Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is a mental health condition that emerges following exposure to traumatic events such as severe accidents, disasters, violence, or military action. It is unique among psychiatric diagnoses due to its clear linkage to external traumatic events.

## The Nature of Traumatic Events

For an event to qualify as a traumatic stressor leading to PTSD, it must involve actual or threatened death, serious injury, or a threat to physical integrity.

## Theories of PTSD

Three main theories attempt to explain the mechanisms behind PTSD:

- **Emotional Processing Theory:** 
  - Suggests that fear structures stored in memory trigger cognitive, behavioral, and physiological reactions upon activation. In PTSD, these fear structures become maladaptive, leading to an overestimation of danger from various stimuli.

- **Dual Representation Theory:**
  - Proposes the existence of different memory types, with situational memories remaining unconscious until triggered, causing PTSD symptoms to manifest.

- **Cognitive Model:**
  - Indicates that negative appraisals of the trauma lead to a worldview that is perceived as dangerous and a self-view of incompetence, causing misinterpretation of situations as threatening.

## Pathophysiology

The exact pathophysiological underpinnings of PTSD are not fully understood, but studies have identified:

- A reduction in hippocampal size.
- Dysregulation of amygdala activity leading to hyper-reactivity to perceived threats and an overly sensitive feedback mechanism.

## Diagnosis of PTSD

The DSM-5 characterizes PTSD by four distinct symptom clusters that must cause significant impairment for a diagnosis:

- **Intrusiveness:**
  - Involves re-experiencing the event through flashbacks, images, and nightmares that are distressing and evoke physiological reactions.

- **Avoidance:**
  - Consists of evading trauma reminders, including people, places, and media that may trigger memories of the event.

- **Negative Alterations in Cognition and Mood:**
  - Can encompass amnesia, pervasive negative beliefs, feelings of blame, and enduring negative emotions such as fear, horror, anger, and guilt.

- **Alterations in Arousal and Reactivity:**
  - Manifest as heightened vigilance, exaggerated startle responses, irritability, angry outbursts, self-destructive behavior, sleep disturbances, and concentration difficulties.

Symptoms must be present for at least one month for a PTSD diagnosis, and the patient's presentation may be delayed. The initial complaint often revolves around re-experiencing symptoms but can range from depression and anxiety to irritability and sleep issues.

## Investigations

The evaluation of PTSD relies on questionnaires and checklists, with no specific blood tests or imaging recommended for diagnosis. Commonly used tools include:

- **PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5):**
  - A 20-question self-report measure assessing the DSM-5 symptoms of PTSD.

- **Impact of Event Scale - Revised (IES-R):**
  - A 22-item scale measuring intrusion, avoidance, and hyper-arousal.

- **Trauma Screening Questionnaire (TSQ):**
  - A brief 10-item questionnaire targeting re-experiencing and arousal symptoms.

## Management of PTSD

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

Recovery may occur without intervention; however, without effective treatment, patients risk developing chronic issues. The approach includes:

- Offering practical, social, and emotional support from healthcare and social workers.
- Ensuring patients are encouraged, reassured, and remain engaged in follow-up.

Psychological therapies such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Trauma-Focused CBT (TFCBT), and Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) are central to treatment.

For severe symptoms or symptoms persisting over 3 months, TFCBT is recommended, typically over 8-12 sessions.

EMDR therapy is another option, focusing on processing the traumatic event and integrating it into the patient's memory, often through bilateral physical stimulation like eye movements or taps, over 8-12 sessions.

### Prescribing Interventions

Antidepressants, especially Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs), can alleviate PTSD symptoms and associated depression. However, SSRIs should ideally be initiated after starting trauma-focused psychological treatment, unless:

- The patient opts out or cannot engage in psychological therapy.
- There is a non-response or intolerance to psychological treatment.
- Psychological treatments are not available.
- There is comorbid depression warranting adjunctive treatment.

The most evidence supports the use of paroxetine, fluoxetine, sertraline, and venlafaxine (an SNRI). Risperidone may augment SSRI treatment for non-responsive cases. Successful pharmacotherapy should continue for at least twelve months, followed by a gradual withdrawal over four weeks to prevent relapse.
