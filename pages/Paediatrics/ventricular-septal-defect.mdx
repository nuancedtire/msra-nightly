# Ventricular Septal Defects (VSD): A Comprehensive Guide

Ventricular Septal Defects (VSD) are second only to bicuspid aortic valves in terms of prevalence among congenital heart defects. VSDs are characterized by holes in the ventricular septum, most commonly found in the membranous septum (perimembranous defects). Less frequently, these defects can be located in the muscular septum.

## Clinical Presentation

Patients with VSD typically exhibit the following symptoms:

* **Loud pansystolic murmur**, which is most profound at the lower left sternal border. This is due to the left-to-right shunting of blood through small VSDs.
* **Tachypnoea** at rest or during feeding, coupled with **failure to thrive** if the VSD is large.
* **Loud S2 and mid-diastolic murmur**, indicative of pulmonary hypertension and high flow through the tricuspid valve.

## Diagnosis

VSD is primarily diagnosed through echocardiography.

## Investigations

The following investigations can further confirm the diagnosis and assess the severity of the condition:

* **Chest X-ray:** Usually normal, but in some cases may show cardiomegaly and increased pulmonary vascular markings.
* **ECG:** Normal for small VSDs; larger VSDs may present signs of ventricular hypertrophy.
* **Echocardiography:** Used for diagnosis, severity assessment, and to check for any associated abnormalities such as aortic regurgitation or pulmonary stenosis.

## Management

The management approach for VSD varies based on the size of the defect.

### Prescribing Management

Small VSDs often do not require treatment, as the majority of patients are asymptomatic and the defect frequently closes spontaneously within the first couple of years of life. These patients do not require follow-up.

For patients with larger VSDs, symptom management may involve:

* Administration of diuretics to alleviate pulmonary congestion.
* Calorie supplementation or even nasogastric tube feeding to support growth and nutrition.

### Non-prescribing Management

Surgery to close a VSD is indicated for patients with large defects that cause symptomatic heart failure and faltering growth. This procedure is generally performed within the first 6 months of life.
