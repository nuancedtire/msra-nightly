# Atopic Eczema: Understanding a Chronic Skin Condition

Atopic eczema is a **chronic inflammatory skin condition** that can affect individuals of all ages, though it is more prevalent in children under the age of 5. It results from a **barrier dysfunction** of the skin and can impact up to **25% of school children** and **2-10% of adults**. This condition is frequently associated with other atopic diseases, including **allergic rhinitis**, **food allergies**, and most notably **asthma**. The eczema typically affects the flexures of the skin, leading to:

- Crusting
- Itchiness
- Peeling of the skin

Atopic eczema can significantly affect mental health and severely decrease the quality of life of those affected.

## Diagnosis

The diagnosis of atopic eczema is primarily **clinical**, relying on the patient's history and physical examination.

### History

- **Age of Onset:** Most patients present between `6 months` and `5 years`.
- Past medical history often includes:
  - **Allergic rhinitis**
  - **Asthma**
  - **Food allergies**

### Symptoms

Common symptoms of atopic eczema include:

- **Dry scaly patches** of skin 
- **Pruritus** – itching is a predominant symptom
- **Lichenification** – accentuation of skin lines
- **Excoriation**

*Note:* Secondary changes like lichenification and excoriation arise from excessive itching.

### Risk Factors

Key risk factors include:

- **Family history** of atopic diseases such as:
  - Allergic rhinitis
  - Food allergy
  - Asthma
  - Atopic eczema
- **Environmental factors** such as:
  - Living in **highly polluted areas**
  - Experiencing **extremes of weather**
- **Exposure** to second-hand smoke
- **High birth weight**

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Important:** Atopic eczema can develop even in the absence of these risk factors, so a lack of risk factors should not rule out diagnosis.
</Callout>

## Investigations

While investigations are not mandatory for diagnosing atopic eczema, they may help confirm the diagnosis:

- **Allergy testing:**
  - Useful for patients with specific allergies to identify allergens to avoid.
  
- **Blood IgE levels:**
  - Many patients may show elevated levels, though this does not alter the treatment approach.
  
- **Skin biopsy:**
  - May be indicated if symptoms and examination findings do not confirm atopic eczema, helping differentiate from conditions like **cutaneous T-cell lymphoma**.

## Management (Prescribing)

Management of atopic eczema requires medication to address both acute flare-ups and the chronic nature of the disease. Key components include:

1. **Emollients:**
   - Essential in both acute and chronic phases, but not sufficient alone for treating acute flare-ups.

2. **Topical Corticosteroids:**
   - Low to medium strength can be prescribed by GPs for flare-ups.
   - Stronger corticosteroids may be required for serious flare-ups, prescribed by dermatologists in secondary care.

3. **Chronic Phase Management:**
   - Emollients combined with topical steroids.
   - Proper guidance on the use and duration of topical steroids is crucial due to potential harmful side effects.

<details>
<summary>Alternative Therapies</summary>

For cases resistant to topical corticosteroids, alternative therapies may include:

- **UV light therapy**
- **Systemic immunosuppressants** such as ciclosporin

These therapies should be prescribed by dermatologists in secondary care.

</details>
