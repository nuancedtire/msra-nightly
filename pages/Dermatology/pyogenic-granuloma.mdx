# Pyogenic Granuloma: An Overview

A **pyogenic granuloma** (also known as **granuloma telangiectaticum**) is a common proliferation of capillary blood vessels, found on both skin and mucosal surfaces. These are **benign growths**, with no reported cases of malignant transformation. However, due to their resemblance to skin cancers, it is often necessary to perform a biopsy and histological analysis, especially in adults or those with significant risk factors.

## Epidemiology

- **Commonly Found In:** 
  - Children (mean age of presentation: 6-7 years)
  - Adults (associated with pregnancy, female sex, and specific medications)
- **Associated Medications:** 
  - Oral contraceptive pills (OCP)
  - Retinoids
  - Chemotherapeutic agents

## Presentation

- **Appearance:**
  - Painless red spot, growing rapidly over days to weeks
  - Typically reaches size of `1-2 cm`
- **Typical Locations:**
  - **Pregnancy-related:** Mucosal surfaces inside the mouth
  - **Other Cases:** Head, neck, and extremities (particularly fingers)

As they grow, pyogenic granulomas may cause discomfort and can bleed profusely or ulcerate due to their vascular nature.

<summary>Common Signs and Symptoms</summary>

- **Growth Characteristics:**
  - Rapid development over a few days.
  - Usually painless unless trauma occurs.

- **Potential Complications:**
  - Bleeding
  - Ulceration
</details>

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis is often straightforward, following a typical clinical course. However, it is crucial to rule out sinister causes, with the most important differential being **amelanocytic melanoma**. Dermatoscopy may be utilized alongside a full clinical history to confirm the diagnosis.

## Investigations

- **Biopsy:** 
  - Essential for histological analysis to rule out malignant causes.

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

### Conservative Management
- **Remove Underlying Cause:** If drug-induced.
- **Watchful Waiting:** Especially for small or pregnancy-related lesions, as they may resolve on their own.

### Surgical Options
- **Excision**
- **Curettage and Cauterisation**
- **Laser Surgery**

### Alternative Treatments
- **Cryotherapy** or **Chemical Cauterisation** with silver nitrate for smaller lesions.
- **Topical Treatments:**
  - Imiquimod cream
  - 1% propranolol ointment (effective in children)

**Recurrence** is common due to deep vascular extension into the dermis; such cases are best managed by surgical excision.
