# Marjolin’s Ulcers: Clinical Insights

Marjolin’s Ulcers are rare tumours of the skin that develop in areas affected by previous injury or chronic inflammation. This primarily includes:

- Burn scars
- Trauma scars
- Pressure sores
- Radiation dermatitis
- Ulcers of venous insufficiency

Although they evolve over a long period, Marjolin’s Ulcers are generally more aggressive than primary skin cancers. Histopathological investigations usually identify these ulcers as *squamous cell carcinomas*, though *basal cell carcinomas* and *malignant melanomas* have also been documented. Due to their extended development period, **atypical presentation** of malignancy, and aggressive nature, these ulcers can often be easily overlooked, yet recognizing them is crucial due to their **poor prognosis**.

## Diagnosis

Marjolin’s Ulcers commonly present at an average age of **59 years**, typically **30-35 years after** the initial injury. They are approximately **three times more common in men** than in women, with the **lower limbs** being the most affected area. One known risk factor for malignant transformation is **immunosuppression**.

### Clinical Presentation

Marjolin’s Ulcers may present with a sudden or unexpected change in an existing scar or chronic wound, characterized by the classic triad of changes:

- **Induration**
- **Ulceration**
- **Nodularity**

Additional changes may include:

- Excess **granulation tissue**
- Excess **exudate**
- **Bleeding**
- **Foul odour**
- Changes in **colour, size, shape,** and **rolled margin contours**

## Investigations

The gold standard for diagnosing Marjolin’s Ulcers is a **biopsy** of the lesion for histological analysis. 

Additional investigations may include:

- **Physical examination** or **Ultrasound imaging** of local lymph nodes to assess potential lymphatic involvement.
- **CT imaging** in cases where metastasis is suspected, commonly affecting the brain, lungs, liver, lymph nodes, and kidneys.

## Management

Management decisions for Marjolin’s Ulcers should take place within a **multidisciplinary team (MDT)** setting. Although there is no **definitive treatment protocol**, typical options include:

- **Wide local excision** or **Mohs surgery**:
  - Mohs surgery permits immediate evaluation of excised tissue to confirm clear margins.
  
- **Amputation** may be necessary in cases of extensive local spread.

- **Adjuvant radiotherapy** or **chemotherapy** can be considered for inoperable or metastatic cases.
