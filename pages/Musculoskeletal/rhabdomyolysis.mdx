# Understanding Rhabdomyolysis

Rhabdomyolysis is an acute medical condition characterized by the breakdown of skeletal muscle fibers, leading to the release of muscle cell contents into the bloodstream. This leakage includes substances such as potassium, phosphate ions, myoglobin, creatine kinase, and urate, which can result in dangerous complications including electrolyte imbalances, acidosis, disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), renal failure, and potentially multi-organ failure.

## Causes of Rhabdomyolysis

Rhabdomyolysis can be triggered by a variety of factors:

- **Alcohol abuse**
- **Muscle overexertion** (e.g., marathon running)
- **Traumatic crush injuries**
- **Genetic disorders**: such as Duchenne’s muscular dystrophy
- **Seizures and status epilepticus**
- **Infections**: including influenza, HIV, and _Legionella_ species
- **Medications**: particularly statins
- **Toxins**: like snakebites
- **Endocrine disorders**: such as hypothyroidism

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Presentation

Diagnosing rhabdomyolysis often starts with clinical suspicion based on patient history and symptoms, which can be non-specific. Patients may exhibit:

- Muscle pain, swelling, and tenderness
- Systemic symptoms such as dark urine, fever, malaise, nausea, and confusion

### Investigations

- **Serum Creatine Kinase**: A key diagnostic marker; levels are typically more than five times the normal range and may exceed 50 times in severe cases.
- **Urine Dipstick**: May show false-positive results for blood due to myoglobin presence.
- **Serum Transaminases**: Often elevated, especially aspartate transaminase (AST).
- **Muscle Biopsy**: Considered when a genetic cause is suspected, especially in recurrent cases.

## Emergency Management

The most severe complication of rhabdomyolysis is acute kidney injury (AKI), which arises from myoglobin obstructing renal tubules.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** AKI requires urgent intervention, following the advanced life support protocol (ABCDE) and addressing complications like fluid overload, hyperkalemia, acidosis, and uremia. Dialysis may be necessary for severe cases.
</Callout>

### Additional Complications

- **Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)**: Characterized by widespread clotting and bleeding due to platelet depletion. Treatment focuses on the underlying cause, but transfusions may be required in rapid-onset cases.
- **Severe Hyperkalemia**: Caused by potassium release from damaged muscles, potentially leading to cardiac arrhythmias. Treatment includes intravenous calcium gluconate to stabilize cardiac membranes, followed by insulin and dextrose infusion to reduce serum potassium.

## Non-Prescriptive Management

The cornerstone of rhabdomyolysis treatment is aggressive hydration to facilitate renal clearance of toxins. Intravenous crystalloids, such as Hartmann's solution, are preferred. Diuretics like furosemide may also be employed to enhance toxin excretion.
