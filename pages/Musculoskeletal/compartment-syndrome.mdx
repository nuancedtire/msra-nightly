# Understanding Compartment Syndrome

Compartment syndrome occurs due to increased tissue pressure within a closed osteofascial space. This condition often arises after injuries such as long bone fractures, hemorrhages, or direct soft-tissue damage. The buildup of pressure restricts capillary blood flow, which, if not addressed swiftly, can lead to tissue death (necrosis).

## Diagnosis

Diagnosing compartment syndrome is primarily clinical and requires a high level of suspicion for prompt identification. The syndrome is characterized by the traditional **6 Ps**:

- **P**ain
- **P**oikilothermia (inability to regulate temperature)
- **P**araesthesia (tingling or numbness)
- **P**aralysis
- **P**ulselessness
- **P**allor (paleness)

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Note:** The last three symptoms—paralysis, pulselessness, and pallor—are rare and usually appear later. The most common signs include disproportionate pain and increased pain with passive movement, along with a sensation of pressure within the affected compartment.
</Callout>

Common sites for compartment syndrome include the leg compartments, particularly the anterior and deep posterior compartments, and the volar compartment of the forearm. These conditions may occur after tibial shaft and supracondylar fractures, respectively.

## Investigations

In cases where diagnosis is uncertain, measuring intracompartmental pressure can be useful. Additionally, serum creatine kinase and urine myoglobin levels should be assessed as they indicate muscle necrosis.

## Management

Compartment syndrome is a medical emergency and requires immediate intervention:

- **Dressing Release**: If the patient is in a cast or has tight dressings, these should be removed immediately.
- **Fasciotomy**: This is the primary treatment for patients without dressings.
- **Supportive Care**: This includes providing analgesics (e.g., morphine) and intravenous fluids.
- **Debridement and Amputation**: In cases where muscle necrosis is evident, as indicated by muscles failing to contract upon electrocoagulation, these procedures may be necessary.

Post-fasciotomy care involves diligent wound management, using skin grafts when necessary, and engaging in physical and occupational therapy. It is also crucial to address the underlying cause, such as a fracture.

<details>
<summary>Detailed Pathophysiology</summary>

Compartment syndrome results from an imbalance between the volume of the compartment and the space available within it. As pressure increases, it surpasses the venous pressure, impeding venous outflow and eventually affecting arterial inflow, leading to ischemia and potential necrosis if untreated.

</details>
