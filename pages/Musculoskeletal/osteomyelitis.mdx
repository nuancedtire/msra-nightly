# Comprehensive Overview of Osteomyelitis

Osteomyelitis is a bone infection that can originate from direct trauma, surgical procedures, or as a complication of nearby infections like cellulitis. Occasionally, the infection spreads from other body areas such as the lungs (pneumonia) or skin abscesses, with no identifiable primary infection source. Common bacteria responsible for osteomyelitis include **Pseudomonas**, **Staphylococcus aureus**, **E.Coli**, and **Streptococci**.

## Types of Osteomyelitis

Osteomyelitis can be classified as either acute or chronic. Chronic osteomyelitis results from untreated or insufficiently treated acute infections, potentially leading to sinus tract formation and infected dead bone (sequestra). X-ray findings may reveal cloaca, which are bone openings that allow pus and necrotic tissue to escape, and involucrum, indicating new bone formation around the sequestrum.

## Diagnosis

### Risk Factors

Individuals at higher risk for developing osteomyelitis include those with:

- Diabetes
- Immunosuppression
- Vascular disease
- Sickle cell disease (*Salmonella* is a common infecting organism)
- Surgical prosthesis or implants
- Intravenous drug use
- Open fractures

In children, osteomyelitis often follows skin lesions or sore throats, targeting the growth plates of long bones.

### Clinical Features of Acute Osteomyelitis

- **Pain and Tenderness**
- **Reduced Movement**: Limited range of motion in the affected area
- **Systemic Symptoms**: Fever, malaise, and night sweats
- **Local Symptoms**: Overlying erythema and effusion

### Cierny-Mader Classification

This classification system categorizes patients based on comorbidities and bone involvement:

- **Comorbidity Categories**:
  - **A**: No comorbidities, likely tolerate antibiotics and surgery
  - **B**: Comorbidities that hinder wound healing, reduce antibiotic efficacy, or complicate surgery
  - **C**: Severe comorbidities make infection treatment more harmful than the condition

- **Bone Involvement Grades**:
  - **I**: Haematogenous spread affecting endosteal and medullary bone
  - **II**: Superficial disease from local infection
  - **III**: Cortical and medullary bone involvement, but some bone remains unaffected
  - **IV**: Diffuse involvement with minimal healthy bone

## Investigations

- **Basic Observations**: Respiratory rate, heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, and oxygen saturation
- **Blood Tests**:
  - **FBC**: May show leucocytosis
  - **CRP**: Elevated levels indicate inflammation
  - **Blood Cultures**: Positive in approximately 60% of osteomyelitis cases
- **X-ray**:
  - Rules out fractures or tumors
  - Shows osteopenia about 7 days post-infection onset
- **MRI**:
  - Provides detailed information on bone and surrounding soft tissues
- **Bone Biopsy/Deep Tissue Specimen**:
  - Guides antibiotic selection

## Management of Emergencies

Swift and aggressive treatment of acute osteomyelitis is crucial to prevent chronic complications, which can include growth restriction in children, joint stiffness, fractures, bone deformity, infection recurrence, and in severe cases, amputation.

## Management (Prescribing)

Upon suspicion of acute osteomyelitis, initiate intravenous antibiotics for 2-4 weeks, followed by oral antibiotics for up to 2 weeks, adhering to local guidelines. Collect blood cultures and a bone biopsy or deep tissue specimen before starting antibiotics. Manage pain with analgesics. If antibiotics fail to elicit a rapid response, or if x-rays indicate progressive destruction, consider surgical intervention to remove sequestra and drain abscesses.

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

Immobilize the affected area. If the lower limbs are involved, avoid full weight-bearing until the infection resolves.
