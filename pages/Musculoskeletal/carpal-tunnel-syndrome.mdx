# Understanding Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Carpal tunnel syndrome is a prevalent neuropathy caused by the compression of the median nerve within the carpal tunnel located at the wrist.

## Diagnosis

Carpal tunnel syndrome is characterized by intermittent pain, numbness, and tingling (paraesthesia) in the hand and fingers, specifically within the sensory distribution area of the median nerve. This area includes:

- The palmar surface of the thumb
- The index finger
- The middle finger
- The radial half of the ring finger

Initially, symptoms tend to exacerbate at night or are triggered by repetitive hand movements. If not addressed, prolonged compression may lead to weakness and muscle wasting in the thenar eminence (the fleshy part of the palm near the thumb).

Several clinical tests can indicate carpal tunnel syndrome if they provoke or worsen paraesthesia in the median nerve distribution:

- **Tinel's Sign**: Tapping over the median nerve at the carpal tunnel.
- **Durkan’s Test**: Applying sustained pressure on the carpal tunnel.
- **Phalen's Maneuver**: Holding wrist flexion for 60 seconds.

## Investigations

To formally confirm a diagnosis of carpal tunnel syndrome, **electrophysiological studies** are necessary. Additionally, specific tests may be conducted to identify any underlying causes of the compression neuropathy, such as:

- Hypothyroidism
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Ganglion cysts
- Traumatic fracture of the distal radius

## Management

Many cases of carpal tunnel syndrome can be effectively managed within primary care settings. It's important to consider **lifestyle modifications**, such as avoiding repetitive hand and finger movements. Wrist splinting and bracing are also beneficial in alleviating symptoms.

For cases that do not respond to initial treatments, **corticosteroid injections** and **surgical decompression** via carpal tunnel release may be necessary.
