# Lumbar Disc Prolapse: A Comprehensive Overview

Lumbar disc prolapse occurs when the nucleus pulposus protrudes through the annulus fibrosus. This condition often results from trauma, such as lifting heavy objects while the spine is flexed, although genetic predispositions may also play a role. Sometimes, activities like coughing or sneezing can trigger a prolapse. When the herniated nucleus pulposus compresses a nerve root, it causes pain radiating to the buttock or leg on one side, known as sciatica. The L4/5 and L5/S1 discs are most frequently affected. While most prolapses occur posterolaterally, a central prolapse can compress the cauda equina, constituting a medical emergency.

## Diagnosis

Lumbar disc prolapse typically presents with localized lumbar pain, which may radiate to the buttock or leg. The onset of pain can be sudden or gradual, often worsening with straining or coughing and alleviating when lying flat.

### Clinical Examination

- **Spine Movement**: Limited extension and forward flexion; unilateral limitation of lateral flexion.
- **Symptom Table**: Effects of spinal root compression on sensory loss, weakness, and reflex loss.

| Disc Level | Root | Sensory Loss       | Weakness             | Reflex Loss |
|------------|------|--------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **L3/4**   | L4   | Inner calf         | Inversion of foot    | Knee        |
| **L4/5**   | L5   | Outer calf, dorsum of foot | Dorsiflexion of toes |             |
| **L5/S1**  | S1   | Sole, lateral foot | Plantar flexion      | Ankle       |

## Investigations

- **MRI Lumbar Spine**: The preferred imaging modality for visualizing soft tissues and intervertebral discs. Generally, reliance on clinical diagnosis is recommended unless pain persists longer than expected.

## Management

### Prescribing

Due to the painful nature of this condition, appropriate analgesia is crucial. If standard medications like paracetamol and NSAIDs are inadequate, consider a short course of weak opioids.

### Non-prescribing

Following a brief period of bed rest (approximately 2 days), encourage the patient to resume normal activities, avoiding heavy lifting. Physiotherapy exercises are recommended to maintain flexibility and mobility. Adequate pain relief is essential to facilitate these activities. In 90% of cases, pain resolves with analgesia and physiotherapy alone. However, if pain persists beyond 6 weeks or there is progressive muscular weakness, a discectomy may be necessary.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Immediate medical attention is required if central prolapse symptoms indicate cauda equina compression.
</Callout>

<details>
<summary>Pathophysiology Details</summary>

The nucleus pulposus, a gel-like center of the disc, can herniate through the annulus fibrosus due to tears or weaknesses in its structure. This protrusion can impinge on nearby nerves, causing varying symptoms based on the affected nerve root.

</details>
