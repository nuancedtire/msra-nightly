# Understanding Osteoarthritis

Osteoarthritis is characterized by the degeneration of joint articular cartilage. This condition commonly affects joints subjected to repetitive or significant stress, leading to progressively worsening pain. Notably, the pain is often more intense at the end of the day or after extended activity.

## Symptoms and Initial Presentation

- **Pain**: Typically increases towards the end of the day or after long periods of activity.
- **Stiffness**: May occur in the morning or after inactivity, generally lasting less than 30 minutes once activity resumes.
- **Commonly Affected Joints**: The first carpometacarpal joint is frequently among the earliest to be impacted.

## Diagnosis

To confirm osteoarthritis or assess its severity, a plain radiograph (x-ray) of the affected joint can be ordered. Radiological examinations often feature in exam questions. The mnemonic **LOSS** is useful for remembering the x-ray changes associated with osteoarthritis:

- **L** – Loss of joint space
- **O** – Osteophytes (bone spurs)
- **S** – Subchondral cysts
- **S** – Subchondral sclerosis

## Management Strategies

### Pharmacological Approaches

- **Analgesics**: For pain relief.
- **NSAIDs**: Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs to reduce inflammation.
- **Opioids**: For severe pain not relieved by other medications.
- **Local Analgesics**: Targeted relief for affected areas.

### Adjunct Therapies

- **Physiotherapy**: To improve joint function and mobility.
- **Intra-articular Joint Injections**: For direct treatment within the joint space.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Note:** Surgical intervention, such as joint replacement, may be considered for severe cases unresponsive to conservative treatment.
</Callout>
