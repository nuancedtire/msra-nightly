# Understanding Crohn's Disease

Crohn's disease (CD) is a chronic inflammatory condition impacting the gastrointestinal tract (GI tract) from the mouth to the perianal area. A hallmark of Crohn's disease is the presence of skip lesions, where healthy sections of the bowel intervene between diseased areas. This unique feature distinguishes it from other inflammatory bowel diseases. Severe inflammation can lead to acute complications like intestinal obstruction and bowel perforations.

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis involves a combination of clinical history, examination, and confirmation through colonoscopy with ileoscopy and tissue biopsy. The nonspecific nature of symptoms often leads to delayed diagnosis. Histories should focus on ruling out other causes like infections.

### Common Symptoms
- Abdominal pain (cramping or constant, relieved by defecation)
- Diarrhea
- Gastrointestinal bleeding
- Weight loss
- Fatigue
- Fever

### Risk Factors
- Family history of inflammatory bowel disease.
- Age: Typically 15-40 years or 60-80 years.
- White ethnicity.
- Ashkenazi Jewish descent (2- to 4-fold increased risk).

### Examination Findings
- **Abdominal:** Right lower quadrant tenderness, palpable mass.
- **Oral:** Ulcers.
- **Perineal:** Skin tags, fistulae, abscesses, sinus tracts.
- **Digital Rectal Exam:** Occult blood, absence of mass.
- **Skin:** Erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum.

## Investigations

Conducting the following first-line tests helps provide clues and confirm diagnosis:

- **Full Blood Count (FBC):** Reveals anemia, leukocytosis, thrombocytosis.
- **Iron Studies:** May indicate deficiency due to gastrointestinal bleeding.
- **Vitamin B12 and Folate Levels:** Indicate malabsorption issues.
- **Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP):** Shows hypoalbuminemia, hypocholesterolemia, hypocalcemia.
- **CRP/ESR:** Inflammatory markers correlate with disease activity.
- **Stool Testing:** Absence of infectious agents supports CD.
- **Abdominal X-ray:** Detects bowel dilation, calcification, sacroiliitis, abscesses.
- **CT abdomen:** Highlights skip lesions, bowel wall thickening, inflammation.
- **MRI abdomen/pelvis:** Superior for assessing disease location, evaluating skip lesions, and related complications.
- **Colonoscopy with Ileoscopy:** Definitive diagnostic test, reveals aphthous ulcers, etc.
- **Tissue Biopsy:** Confirms diagnosis with histological evidence of transmural involvement and non-caseating granulomas.

## Emergency Management

### Intestinal Obstruction

- **Cause:** Chronic inflammation leads to fibrosis, causing strictures.
- **Symptoms:** Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, distension, altered bowel habits.
- **Examination:** Diffuse tenderness, altered bowel sounds.
- **Investigation:** Abdominal x-ray shows dilated loops, air-fluid levels.
- **Treatment:** 
  - Partial: Nasogastric decompression, corticosteroids.
  - Complete: Surgical intervention.

### Bowel Perforation

- **Cause:** Fragility from inflammation or obstruction.
- **Symptoms:** Sharp abdominal pain, systemic symptoms like malaise, vomiting.
- **Examination:** Sepsis signs, peritonism.
- **Investigation:** Erect chest x-ray reveals free air under diaphragm.
- **Treatment:**
  - Initiate sepsis management plan.
  - Surgery often required depending on perforation site and patient's condition.

## Management (Prescribing)

Management of Crohn's therapies requires specialists familiar with treatment complexities, side effects, and monitoring requirements. Treatment aims to maintain remission and relieve symptoms. Refer to the [European Crohn’s and Colitis Organisation guidelines](https://academic.oup.com/ecco-jcc/article/11/1/3/2456546) for detailed recommendations.

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

Around 50% of Crohn's patients undergo surgical intervention when symptoms persist despite optimal medical treatment or when complications arise. **Smoking cessation** is the sole lifestyle change proven to reduce Crohn's recurrence.
