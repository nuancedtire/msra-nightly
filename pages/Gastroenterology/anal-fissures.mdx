# Understanding Anal Fissures

Anal fissures are tears in the skin of the distal anal canal, often causing significant pain during defecation and bleeding, known as hematochezia, typically observed as blood on toilet paper. These fissures may arise due to increased anal sphincter tone caused by trauma or underlying conditions, severely affecting a patient’s quality of life. Despite many individuals experiencing them multiple times in their lives, treatment is generally conservative, with surgery being rare.

## Diagnosis

Anal fissures are diagnosed primarily through clinical assessment based on patient history, with additional tests seldom required.

### Symptoms
- Pain during defecation
- Tearing or burning sensation while passing stool
- Sensation of "passing glass"
- Fresh blood on stool or toilet paper

### Risk Factors
- Sexually transmitted diseases like syphilis or herpes
- Crohn's disease
- Anal cancer
- Psoriasis
- Hard stool
- Chronic constipation
- Use of opiate analgesia
- Pregnancy

These risk factors increase the likelihood of developing anal fissures. If an underlying condition is suspected, further investigation, such as a Digital Rectal Exam (DRE), could be beneficial in specific scenarios, despite patients often fearing the associated discomfort.

## Investigations

Anal fissures rarely require first-line investigations as they are mainly diagnosed clinically. However, persistent cases unresponsive to lifestyle changes and conservative treatment may necessitate further analysis.

### Anal Manometry
- Measures resting pressures of the anal sphincter muscles, which may show low resting pressure in chronic fissure patients.

### Anal Ultrasound
- Endoanal ultrasound helps evaluate sphincter defects potentially contributing to fissures.

## Management (Prescribing)

Managing pain is essential due to its prominence in anal fissure cases. Opt for paracetamol and ibuprofen while avoiding opiate analgesia to prevent constipation worsening. Topical anesthetics like lidocaine can alleviate pain during defecation. Dietary interventions may include:
- Stool softeners or laxatives to ease stool passage
- **Topical treatments**: Glyceryl trinitrate or diltiazem for both children and adults, not recommended for pregnant/lactating women
- **Further interventions**: Botulinum toxin injections, especially beneficial post-childbirth for women

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

Lifestyle changes can significantly resolve anal fissures:
- **Dietary fiber increase**: Encourage intake of whole grains, fruits, and vegetables to facilitate stool passage and reduce strain.
- **Hydration**: Maintaining proper fluid intake supports easier stool passage.
- **Sitz baths**: Warm, shallow baths can offer pain relief.

### Surgical Options
- **Surgical Sphincterotomy**: Involves cutting/stretching the internal sphincter to lower resting pressure; requires thorough second-line investigations.
- **Anal Advancement Flap**: An alternative to sphincterotomy, less frequently performed.

<summary>Detailed Pathophysiology</summary>

Anal fissures often develop due to high anal sphincter tone, which can result from trauma or diseases like Crohn's or psoriasis. While commonly managed conservatively, complex or chronic fissures may need surgical intervention.
