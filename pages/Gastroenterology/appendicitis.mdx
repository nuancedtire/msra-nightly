# Understanding Appendicitis

Appendicitis refers to the inflammation of the vermiform appendix and is one of the primary reasons for emergency abdominal surgery globally. Untreated appendicitis can progress to localized ischemia, perforation, and can result in a contained abscess or generalized peritonitis.

## Epidemiology and Symptoms

Appendicitis is most prevalent in people aged 20-30 years, with an occurrence rate of approximately 223 per 100,000 individuals. The typical symptoms include:

- **Right anterior iliac fossa pain**
- **Anorexia**
- **Nausea and Vomiting**

It's important to note that appendicitis can often present atypically. Only about 50-60% of patients experience the classic migratory pain from the periumbilical area to the right anterior iliac fossa. Early symptoms may include:

- Indigestion
- Flatulence
- Bowel irregularity or diarrhea
- General malaise

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Note:** Early signs like low-grade fever and equivocal abdominal examination may be subtle.
</Callout>

As inflammation worsens, local tenderness in the right lower quadrant (RLQ) and a high-grade fever typically develop. Physical signs to assess include:

- **McBurney's point tenderness**: One-third of the distance from the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) to the umbilicus is notably tender.
- **Rovsing's sign**: Pain in the RLQ upon palpation of the LLQ.
- **Psoas sign**: RLQ pain with passive right hip extension, especially if the appendix is retrocecal.
- **Obturator sign**: Pain upon internal rotation of the right hip with the hip and knee flexed, indicating a pelvic appendix.

### Alvarado Score

The **Alvarado score** aids in identifying the likelihood of appendicitis. The scoring elements include:

| Criteria                          | Points |
|-----------------------------------|--------|
| Migratory RLQ pain                | 1      |
| Anorexia                          | 1      |
| Nausea or vomiting                | 1      |
| RLQ tenderness                    | 2      |
| Rebound tenderness in RLQ         | 1      |
| Fever >37.5°C                      | 1      |
| Leucocytosis >10 x 10<sup>9</sup>/L | 2      |

A total score of 0-3 suggests appendicitis is less likely.

## Investigations

- **Blood Tests**: Elevated white blood cell counts and C-reactive protein (CRP) levels are common, though they may be mild early on.
- **Pregnancy Test**: Recommended for women of childbearing potential.
- **Imaging**: 
  - Computed Tomography (CT) is preferred in adults for its high diagnostic precision.
  - Ultrasound (US) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) are alternatives for children and pregnant women due to concerns over radiation exposure.
  - Plain X-ray generally offers limited diagnostic benefit.

## Emergency Management

In cases where the appendix perforates, patients may require urgent medical intervention. Treatment varies based on stability:

- **Unstable Perforation**: Immediate appendectomy and intravenous (IV) antibiotics are essential.
- **Stable Perforation and Abscess**: Managed with IV antibiotics and either percutaneous drainage or appendectomy. If drainage is performed, a follow-up appendectomy is recommended to exclude neoplasia.

## Management Strategies

### Prescribing

- Employ broad-spectrum antibiotics targeting gram-negative rods and anaerobes. Selection should align with local guidelines.

### Non-Prescribing

- While antibiotics might suffice for uncomplicated cases, appendectomy remains the definitive treatment. This procedure is typically performed laparoscopically in most Western healthcare facilities where feasible.

<details>
<summary>Detailed Pathophysiology</summary>
 
Appendicitis results from blockage of the appendix lumen, leading to increased pressure, impaired blood flow, and subsequent infection. This can escalate to tissue necrosis and rupture.

</details>
