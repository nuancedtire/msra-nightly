# Cluster Headaches: Diagnosis and Management

Cluster headaches are a relatively rare yet extremely painful primary headache disorder, affecting about 1 in 1000 individuals. This condition exhibits a strong male predominance and typically first manifests in the third and fourth decades of life.

## Overview of Cluster Headaches

Cluster headaches are characterized by their chronic nature and severe impact on the quality of life. They are defined by the following features:

- **Prevalence:** Approximately 1 in 1000 individuals.
- **Demographics:** Higher incidence in males, commonly presenting in the 30s and 40s.
- **Pain Episodes:** Intense, recurrent headaches occurring in clusters, lasting from 15 minutes to several hours.
- **Frequency:** Multiple headache episodes throughout a day.
- **Cycles:** Active periods ranging from days to a year, interspersed with remission phases of weeks or months.
- **Diagnosis:** Largely clinical, based on the pattern and characteristics of the headaches.

Patients experience debilitating pain episodes multiple times a day during active periods. Diagnosis is primarily based on clinical presentation, and while additional investigations may be necessary to exclude other conditions, they are not required for diagnosis.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptoms

The hallmark of cluster headaches is the excruciatingly severe unilateral pain that targets the orbital, supraorbital, and temporal regions. Key characteristics include:

- **Pain Quality:** Described as drilling or piercing in nature.
- **Duration:** Abrupt onset and termination of pain.
- **Associated Symptoms:** Include:
  - Excessive tearing (lacrimation)
  - Nasal congestion and discharge (rhinorrhoea)
  - Forehead and facial sweating
  - Pupil constriction (miosis)
  - Eyelid drooping (ptosis)

### Differential Diagnosis

Distinguishing cluster headaches from migraines is crucial, as migraines can present with similar auras. Notable differences are:

- **Onset and Duration:** Cluster headaches are more acute and shorter in duration.
- **Temporal Patterns:** Cluster headaches have distinct patterns of recurrence.

## Investigations

Investigations aim to rule out other headache causes, such as space-occupying lesions or infections. The following may be considered:

- **Imaging:** CT scans or MRI for brain lesions.
- **Blood Tests:** Full Blood Count (FBC) and Urea & Electrolytes (U&Es;) for infection and renal function.
- **Lumbar Puncture:** For meningitis, subarachnoid haemorrhage, or idiopathic intracranial hypertension.

## Management Strategies

### Pharmacological Interventions

- **Abortive Treatments:**
  - Triptans: Administered subcutaneously, intranasally, or orally for rapid relief.
  - Lidocaine: Topical application as an adjunct for acute attacks.
- **Preventative Treatments:**
  - Prednisolone: Short-term, in tapering doses for clusters.
  - Methysergide: Useful for prolonged clusters but carries risks of fibrosis.
  - Ergotamine: Preemptive use 1 hour before predicted attacks, not in combination with triptans.
- **Long-term Prevention:**
  - Verapamil: Requires regular ECG monitoring.
  - Lithium: Needs careful monitoring for serum levels and renal/liver function.

### Non-pharmacological Interventions

- **Oxygen Therapy:**
  - Effective for acute attacks, using a non-rebreather mask at 7-12 L/min for 20 minutes.
- **Trigger Avoidance:**
  - Recognizing and avoiding triggers such as alcohol, GTN sprays, stress, and exercise is essential.

<details>
<summary>Experimental Treatments</summary>

Some patients may explore experimental treatments like occipital nerve blocks or deep brain stimulation, although evidence for their effectiveness is limited.

</details>
