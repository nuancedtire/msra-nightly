# Papilloedema: Diagnosis and Management

Papilloedema is the swelling of the optic disc due to increased intracranial pressure, which can cause optic nerve damage. Recognizing and treating papilloedema promptly is crucial as it can be a life-threatening emergency.

## Causes of Papilloedema

Papilloedema develops due to various underlying conditions that lead to raised intracranial pressure. It is typically bilateral and evolves over a period ranging from hours to weeks. The causes include:

- Hypercapnia
- Malignant hypertension
- Idiopathic intracranial hypertension
- Space-occupying lesions

## Symptoms and Signs

Patients with papilloedema may exhibit a range of symptoms and signs, such as:

- Double vision (**Diplopia**)
- Changes in visual acuity
- Headaches, often more severe in the morning
- Vomiting
- Loss of consciousness

### Alternative Diagnoses

It is important to distinguish papilloedema from other conditions that may present with similar ocular findings:

- Congenitally crowded optic discs
- Optic disc drusen
- Tilted optic discs

## Diagnostic Investigations

A detailed patient history and physical examination should precede specific ophthalmic assessments, including:

- **Fundal Examination** to inspect the back of the eye
- **Visual Acuity** testing
- **Ocular Motility** assessment
- **Pupillary Examination**
- **Visual Fields** assessment

During the examination, signs indicative of papilloedema may include:

- Absence of the optic cup
- Paton’s lines (radial retinal lines emanating from the optic disc)
- Blurred margins of the optic disc
- Elevation of the optic disc
- Reduced or absent venous pulsation
- Venous engorgement

The severity of papilloedema is graded based on these clinical signs. In early stages, one might observe normal visual acuity and pupillary responses, accompanied by venous engorgement and disc pinkness.

Imaging tests such as a CT scan or MRI are essential to exclude intracranial lesions, hemorrhages, infarctions, or hydrocephalus. A lumbar puncture, performed after excluding an intracranial mass, can help assess cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) pressure.

## Managing Emergencies

Rapid diagnosis and intervention are critical to prevent irreversible consequences like vision loss and optic nerve atrophy.

- Patients with signs of increased intracranial pressure should be urgently referred to a neurologist for immediate treatment.
- Asymptomatic patients still require urgent ophthalmological or neurological evaluation.

## Management Strategies

### Prescribing Treatment

For idiopathic intracranial hypertension diagnosed after unremarkable CT/MRI results and spinal CSF taps, treatment options include:

- **Acetazolamide** to decrease CSF production
- **Diuretics**
- Pain relief with medications like **topiramate**, which may also aid in weight loss if it contributes to the condition

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

Surgical intervention might be required for papilloedema resulting from space-occupying lesions or similar pathologies. Post-surgical resolution of papilloedema can take 6-8 weeks. Tumor biopsies and additional treatments like radiotherapy may follow. In cases of idiopathic intracranial hypertension, repeated spinal taps may be necessary, and symptoms may take longer to resolve. Weight loss can also benefit these patients.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Once vision loss begins, it can become permanent within days without effective treatment.
</Callout>
