# Understanding Epilepsy: Diagnosis and Management

Epilepsy is a widely prevalent and variable neurological disorder characterized by the occurrence of recurrent, unprovoked seizures. The condition can manifest through numerous sub-categories and specific syndromes, affecting approximately 1 in 200 individuals in the UK.

The etiology of epilepsy is diverse, often idiopathic with a genetic element, but it can also arise from cerebrovascular disease, brain tumors, excessive alcohol consumption, and traumatic brain injuries. Despite the challenges, epilepsy is often well-managed with anti-epileptic drugs (AEDs), although poorly controlled epilepsy is linked to significant morbidity and reduced quality of life.

## Diagnosis of Epilepsy

Epilepsy is diagnosed through a combination of clinical assessment, supportive investigations, and the exclusion of other seizure causes.

### Seizure Classification

Seizures are generally categorized into two types:

- **Generalised Seizures:** Result from widespread electrical disturbances across the brain. Examples include tonic-clonic, myoclonic, and absence seizures.
- **Focal Seizures:** Originate from a specific brain region and are sub-divided into:
  - Simple seizures where consciousness is retained.
  - Complex seizures involving loss of awareness.

### Clinical Features

Epileptic seizures exhibit distinctive characteristics such as:

- Tongue-biting
- Incontinence
- Tonic-clonic limb movements
- Sensory or olfactory auras preceding the seizure
- Post-ictal fatigue or confusion

Patients might experience various seizure types, but each will typically present with a consistent pattern during occurrences.

### Diagnostic Process

Diagnosis is conducted in neurology clinics via:

- Detailed seizure history, often requiring collateral accounts and video evidence
- Neurological examination to identify any focal neurological signs indicative of structural brain abnormalities

## Investigations

Although clinical evaluation is paramount, investigations are essential to rule out alternative seizure causes and establish treatment baselines.

### Key Investigations

- **Electroencephalogram (EEG) and Sleep Studies:** Aid in epilepsy sub-classification and confirm diagnoses.
- **Blood Tests:** Include Full Blood Count (FBC), Urea & Electrolytes (U&Es), Liver Function Tests (LFTs), Calcium profile, and Glucose levels.
- **Imaging:** MRI scans are warranted if structural lesions are suspected, particularly for temporal lobe seizures potentially caused by mesial temporal sclerosis.

## Emergency Diagnosis and Management

### Status Epilepticus

Prolonged refractory seizures, known as status epilepticus, are a medical emergency requiring immediate attention.

- **Definition:** Continuous seizures or multiple seizures without full recovery in between, lasting beyond 30 minutes.
- **Management:** Follows Advanced Life Support guidelines with an ABCDE approach, prioritizing airway management and administering rectal diazepam or IV lorazepam, progressing to other drugs as needed.

**Warning:** Always monitor vital signs continuously during the procedure.
</Callout>

### Education and First Aid

Patients and families must be educated about the risk of status epilepticus and provided with first aid guidelines and rescue medications. They are instructed to seek emergency help if a seizure exceeds 5 minutes.

### SUDEP

Sudden Unexplained Death in Epilepsy (SUDEP) is a rare but critical risk where the patient dies during or after a seizure. Risk minimization involves strict disease control.

## Management

### Medication

Anti-epileptic drugs are the cornerstone of epilepsy management, aiming for complete seizure control. Medication initiation should occur post-diagnosis, not after a single seizure event.

#### First-Line AEDs

- **Sodium Valproate:** Preferred for generalized seizures.
- **Lamotrigine:** Suitable for focal seizures and women of childbearing age.
- **Carbamazepine:** Another first-line option.

### Non-Medication Strategies

Lifestyle and educational approaches are integral, focusing on:

- Trigger avoidance (alcohol, sleep deprivation, stress)
- Safety measures (guards on fireplaces, avoiding high-risk solo activities)
- Compliance with driving regulations (informing DVLA, adhering to driving restrictions)

### Surgical and Other Interventions

Surgery and vagal nerve stimulation are considered for cases with identifiable resectable lesions or refractory epilepsy not amenable to medical management.
