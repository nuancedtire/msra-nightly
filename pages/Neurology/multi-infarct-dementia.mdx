# Vascular Dementia: Diagnosis and Management

Vascular dementia, also known as multi-infarct dementia, is the second most prevalent type of dementia following Alzheimer's disease. This condition is characterized by cognitive decline due to decreased blood flow to the brain, leading to brain tissue loss. Infarcts, subcortical leukoencephalopathy, or hemorrhage typically cause this reduced blood supply. Commonly associated with vascular risk factors, vascular dementia can be exacerbated by conditions such as diabetes mellitus, atherosclerosis, or hypertension.

## Diagnosis

### Criteria for Diagnosis
Diagnosing vascular dementia involves identifying dementia symptoms alongside evidence of cerebrovascular disease. Dementia is broadly defined by memory impairment coupled with at least two additional cognitive deficits:

- Orientation
- Attention
- Language
- Visuospatial functions
- Executive functions

### Symptomatology
Patients may exhibit a variety of symptoms, including:

- Dysphasia, either receptive or expressive
- Problem-solving difficulties
- Disorientation in time and place
- Challenges in learning new information
- Struggles with daily activities, such as eating, domestic tasks, and self-care
- Behavioral and psychological issues like psychosis, agitation, depression, and disruptions in sleep patterns

### Neurological and Imaging Evidence
Evidence supporting a cerebrovascular disease diagnosis may include:

- Patient history
- Focal signs from neurological evaluations (e.g., hemiparesis, visual field defects, facial weakness)
- Brain imaging results

### Clinical Features Specific to Vascular Dementia
Certain clinical characteristics suggest probable vascular dementia:

- Sudden symptom onset
- Stepwise progression of cognitive deficits
- Early gait dysfunction or instability
- Early onset of urinary incontinence
- Cognitive symptoms becoming evident within `<3 months` of a stroke

## Investigations

While vascular dementia is diagnosed clinically, additional tests help rule out other cognitive impairment causes:

- Full Blood Count (FBC) for anemia
- Vitamin B12 levels to detect B12 deficiency
- Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs) for hypothyroidism
- Electrolytes for hyponatremia

Brain scans such as CT head or MRI are essential to identify infarcts, leukoencephalopathy, or hemorrhages that contribute to the development of vascular dementia.

## Management

### Prescribing Interventions
No cure exists for vascular dementia; instead, management focuses on preventing further atherosclerotic disease by controlling cardiovascular risk factors. Treatment typically includes:

- Antiplatelets (aspirin, clopidogrel)
- Antihypertensives (e.g., ramipril)
- Statins therapy
- Optimizing glycemic control in patients with diabetes mellitus

Patients may also need medications for Alzheimer's disease, such as cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil) or NMDA antagonists (memantine).

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

Non-pharmacological interventions can enhance cognitive symptoms:

- Structured group cognitive stimulation programs
- Reminiscence therapy
- Utilization of familiar objects from the past or life histories

A carotid artery stenosis `>70%` can be treated with carotid endarterectomy or angioplasty and stenting to reduce further stroke risk.
