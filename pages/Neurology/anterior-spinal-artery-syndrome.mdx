# Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome: An Overview

## Introduction

Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome (ASAS) is a neurological condition characterized by ischemia of the anterior spinal artery, which diminishes blood flow to the anterior two-thirds of the spinal cord. This syndrome notably impacts the spinothalamic tract, corticospinal tract, and autonomic fibers, resulting in a spectrum of neurological deficits.

## Pathophysiology

The compromised blood supply typically arises from:

- Atherosclerotic disease
- Thrombosis
- Aortic aneurysms
- Aortic dissections
- Traumatic injury

Consequently, patients may experience:

- Loss of pain and temperature sensations
- Paralysis of voluntary motor function
- Autonomic dysfunction, including hypotension

ASAS is the most prevalent spinal cord infarct, yet it carries a high mortality rate and often leaves survivors with permanent disabilities.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Presentation

Symptoms typically emerge within an hour of ischemia onset and may include:

- Acute back pain at the lesion site
- Motor paralysis below the lesion level
- Loss of pain and temperature sensation
- Autonomic dysfunction (e.g., hypotension, bladder/bowel/sexual dysfunction)
- Preservation of light touch, vibration, and proprioception 

### Diagnostic Evaluation

A thorough history and neurological examination are paramount. Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is the definitive diagnostic tool, although it may not reveal abnormalities within the first 24 hours. Clinical suspicion remains crucial for early diagnosis. Differential diagnoses such as cauda equina syndrome or spinal cord compression can be ruled out using MRI.

## Investigations

The MRI of the spine is the gold standard for confirming ASAS, showcasing:

- Hyperintensity of the anterior horns on T2-weighted images, known as the "owl-eyes sign"
- Potential underlying etiological factors

Additional tests may include:

- Routine blood tests
- Advanced serology for infections such as Lyme disease, syphilis, HIV, herpesviruses
- Lumbar puncture for cerebrospinal fluid analysis

## Management

### Prescribing Interventions

Immediate intervention is vital for ASAS, which is considered a neurological emergency. Treatment strategies include:

- Thrombolysis within 4.5 hours of symptom onset
- Antiplatelet therapy
- Intravenous fluids or vasopressors to manage hypotension
- Long-term anticoagulation to prevent thromboembolic events

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

Supportive care is integral to management, involving:

- Mechanical prophylaxis for thromboembolism prevention
- Physiotherapy and occupational therapy for rehabilitation
- Potential bladder catheterization for bladder dysfunction management

Patients with cervical lesions may require:

- Mechanical ventilation due to respiratory muscle impairment

## Conclusion

Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome remains a severe condition with a high risk of permanent disability. Comprehensive management encompasses both acute medical interventions and long-term rehabilitative strategies.
