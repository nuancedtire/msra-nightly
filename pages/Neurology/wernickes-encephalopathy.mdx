# Wernicke’s Encephalopathy: Diagnosis, Management, and Prevention

Wernicke’s encephalopathy (WE) is a neurological and psychiatric condition characterized by three distinct symptoms: confusion, eye movement issues (ophthalmoplegia), and unsteady gait (ataxia). This condition stems from a deficiency in thiamine (vitamin B1) and is notably linked to alcohol misuse, which impedes thiamine absorption. Moreover, malnutrition, anorexia nervosa, hyperemesis gravidarum, and bowel resection can also lead to thiamine deficiency.

When untreated, WE may progress to Korsakoff's syndrome, a chronic and worsening cognitive impairment primarily associated with long-term thiamine deficiency.

## Diagnosis

### Recognizing Clinical Features

- **Clinical Triad**:
  - Acute confusional state
  - Ophthalmoplegia
  - Ataxia

<Callout emoji="ℹ️">
**Note:** Diagnosis does not require the presence of all three symptoms; in fact, only about 10% of patients exhibit the complete triad. Confusion is the most common symptom.
</Callout>

- **Risk Factors**:
  - History of excessive alcohol consumption
  - Nutritional deficiencies
  - Medical conditions leading to malabsorption

### Diagnostic Approach

- **Clinical Judgment**: Diagnosis is largely based on characteristic symptoms in patients with known risk factors.
- **Imaging**: Cranial CT and MRI scans are not typically useful for diagnosing WE.

## Investigations

While WE is clinically diagnosed, certain tests are performed to exclude other potential causes of the presenting symptoms.

- **Blood Tests**:
  - Full blood count (FBC)
  - Urea and electrolytes (U&Es)
  - Liver function tests (LFTs)
  - Calcium levels

- **Urinalysis**:
  - To detect urinary tract infections (UTIs)
  - Urinary drug screen

- **Cranial Imaging**:
  - Conducted if neurological causes such as infections, stroke, or tumors are suspected

## Management

### Prescribing Treatment

**Immediate Thiamine Replacement**:
- Intravenous thiamine (e.g., Pabrinex)
  - Dosage: 2 pairs administered three times daily for 3-7 days

**Prophylactic Treatment**:
- High-risk patients (history of alcoholism or malnutrition) may receive thiamine prophylactically, especially upon hospital admission.
- Oral thiamine supplements for those at risk but asymptomatic, potentially as a long-term preventive measure.

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

- **Addressing Underlying Causes**:
  - Treatment plans should include strategies for managing alcohol dependence, such as referrals to support services and psychological therapies.

- **Rehabilitation and Support**:
  - Patients may require physiotherapy or occupational therapy for lingering ataxia.
  - Full-time care may be necessary for individuals with Korsakoff’s syndrome due to irreversible cognitive impairments.

<Callout emoji="✅">
**Positive Outcomes**: With timely thiamine treatment, confusion and ophthalmoplegia often resolve quickly, though ataxia may persist.
</Callout>
