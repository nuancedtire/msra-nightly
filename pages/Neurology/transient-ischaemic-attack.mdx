# Transient Ischemic Attack: Management and Regulations

## Overview of TIA
Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA) is characterized by a temporary period of neurological dysfunction due to focal brain ischemia, lasting less than 24 hours. Because of the significant risk of subsequent stroke, a TIA is considered a medical emergency. Immediate and secondary prevention alongside modification of risk factors is crucial.

## Acute Management of TIA
- Initial treatment for suspected TIA includes the administration of 300mg Aspirin orally, except when:
  - The patient has a bleeding disorder.
  - The patient is on anticoagulant therapy.
  - Aspirin is contraindicated for other reasons.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** In cases where Aspirin is not suitable, seek specialist advice without delay.
</Callout>

- Patients with a recent TIA (within the past week) require urgent referral to a specialist for evaluation within 24 hours.

## Driving Regulations Post-TIA
- The Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency (DVLA) mandates a minimum one-month cessation of driving following a TIA or stroke.
  
<details>
<summary>Assessment for Resuming Driving</summary>

Before resuming driving, a doctor must confirm the absence of neurological symptoms, particularly visual or cognitive impairments.

</details>

- Notification to the DVLA is not required after full recovery from a TIA or stroke, except for holders of bus, coach, or lorry licenses.
- If problems persist beyond one month, inform the DVLA regardless of license type.
