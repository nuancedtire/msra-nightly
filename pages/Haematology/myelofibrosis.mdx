# A Comprehensive Overview of Splenomegaly

## Introduction

Splenomegaly refers to the enlargement of the spleen. This condition can result from various underlying factors, including **haematological**, **infectious**, **neoplastic**, **congestive**, or **connective tissue disorders**.

## Causes of Splenomegaly

There are multiple causes of splenomegaly, which can be broadly categorized into different groups:

- **Haematological Causes:**
  - Includes infiltration by cancer cells from haematological malignancies.

- **Infectious Causes:**
  - *Infectious Mononucleosis* is among the most common infectious causes.

- **Tumours and Cysts:**
  - Abnormal growths can also lead to splenic enlargement.

- **Congestive Causes:**
  - Portal hypertension secondary to liver disease is a notable congestive cause.

- **Connective Tissue Disorders and Others:**
  - Various connective tissue disorders may contribute to splenomegaly.

## Symptoms of Splenomegaly

Individuals with an enlarged spleen may experience a range of symptoms or none at all:

- **Asymptomatic in Some Cases**
- **Left Upper Quadrant Pain:** 
  - Some patients report pain or discomfort in this region.
- **Fullness and Early Satiety:** 
  - An uncomfortable feeling of fullness or early satiety can occur.

Symptoms might also be directly related to the underlying cause of the splenomegaly.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Note:** Abnormal laboratory findings often accompany splenomegaly and may include altered haemoglobin, WBC, platelet counts, ALP, and bilirubin levels.
</Callout>

## Conclusion

Splenomegaly is an important clinical finding that necessitates a careful evaluation of potential underlying causes. A comprehensive understanding of the symptoms and associated laboratory abnormalities can guide effective management.
