# Comprehensive Guide to Folic Acid Deficiency

Folic acid deficiency, often occurring alongside vitamin B12 deficiency, is a prevalent cause of megaloblastic anemia, affecting approximately 5% of the global population.

## Diagnosis

Folic acid deficiency can arise from various causes, with insufficient dietary intake being predominant, often due to restrictive diets, alcoholism, or poverty. Other causes include:

- **Malabsorption:** Conditions such as coeliac or Crohn's disease.
- **Increased Demand:** Particularly during pregnancy.
- **Other Conditions:** Such as haemolysis, inflammatory diseases, malignancy, renal or liver dysfunction, and certain medications (e.g., anti-epileptics, trimethoprim, methotrexate).

**Common signs and symptoms:**

- Malaise
- Pallor of mucous membranes and nail beds
- Anorexia and weight loss
- Tachycardia and mild fever
- Jaundice and weakness

**Additional possible symptoms:**

- **Liver and Heart:** Liver enlargement, heart murmurs, angina.
- **Mouth and Skin:** Ulcers in the oropharyngeal region and on the tongue, brown pigmentation of skin and nail beds.
- **Cognitive and Respiratory:** Cognitive changes, dyspnoea, headaches, indigestion.

*Differential diagnosis* should consider:

- **Nutritional and Lifestyle Factors:** Vitamin B12 deficiency, alcoholism, excessive smoking.
- **Haematological and Hormonal Conditions:** Myelodysplasia, aplastic anemia, chronic liver disease, severe thyroid deficiency.

## Investigations

- **Full Blood Count:** Reveals low haemoglobin, elevated mean corpuscular volume (MCV `> 100` femtolitres), and in severe cases, reduced platelet and white blood cell count.
- **Blood Film:** Shows hypersegmented neutrophils and oval macrocytes.
- **Serum Folate:** Levels below 3 micrograms/L (or 7 nanomol/L) may suggest folic acid deficiency.
- **Red Cell Folate:** Less than 150 micrograms/L (or 340 nanomol/L) is a more reliable deficiency indicator.
- **B12 Serum Level:** Consider concurrent vitamin B12 deficiency requiring dual supplementation.

## Management (Prescribing)

For patients with dietary deficiencies or treatable underlying conditions, a daily oral dose of 5mg folic acid for 4 months is generally sufficient. In cases of untreatable causes, lifelong supplementation may be necessary. If vitamin B12 deficiency is present, supplement both folic acid and vitamin B12 to prevent neurological complications.

<Callout emoji="ðŸ“">
**Note for Pregnant Women:** 
Women intending to conceive or who are pregnant should take 400 micrograms of folic acid daily until the twelfth week of pregnancy to prevent neural tube defects. High-risk groups should take 5mg during pregnancy planning and until the twelfth week.
</Callout>

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

Incorporate folate-rich foods into the diet including:

- **Vegetables:** Brussels sprouts, broccoli, asparagus, peas.
- **Others:** Yeast, brown rice, nuts, chickpeas.

For patients with alcohol-related deficiencies, reducing alcohol consumption is advised.
