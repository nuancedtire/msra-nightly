# Understanding Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA) is a prevalent form of anemia characterized by insufficient iron levels. This condition arises due to various causes including:

- **Reduced iron intake**
- **Increased iron requirements**
- **Loss of iron**

IDA affects approximately 20% of women, with increased prevalence among women of childbearing age, children, and individuals in middle or low-income countries. In women of reproductive age, the condition is often linked to menstruation and childbirth, which heighten iron needs and lead to greater iron loss.

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis of IDA combines clinical evaluation and laboratory tests. Key symptoms and signs of IDA include:

- Fatigue
- Headaches
- Exercise intolerance
- Weakness
- Pica
- Restless Leg Syndrome
- Exertional Dyspnea
- Koilonychia
- Cheilosis (Angular Cheilitis)
- Atrophic Glossitis

## Investigations

Essential tests for diagnosing IDA are a complete blood count and iron studies. It's crucial to exclude apparent causes like bleeding, such as slow gastrointestinal bleeds. A typical diagnostic result includes:

- Decreased Hemoglobin (Hb) and hematocrit
- Decreased Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV)
- **Peripheral blood smear**: Shows microcytic, hypochromic cells
- Decreased Reticulocyte count
- Decreased Serum Iron
- **Increased Total Iron-Binding Capacity (TIBC)**
- Decreased Transferrin Saturation
- Decreased Serum Ferritin

Additional evaluations to consider:

- Coeliac Disease Screen
- Urinalysis
- Upper Gastrointestinal Endoscopy
- Lower Gastrointestinal Endoscopy

## Management (Prescribing)

Treatment relies on lab results and symptom severity, emphasizing practicality to avoid unnecessary medication. Challenges with iron supplements, like gastrointestinal discomfort, are common.

For mild IDA with minimal symptoms and slight blood changes, dietary assessment can ensure sufficient iron intake. When difficulty arises, oral iron supplements are an option. Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) enhances non-heme iron absorption from plant-based diets. In cases where oral iron is intolerable, leading to potential complications, intravenous iron may be administered, subject to senior approval.

In severe IDA cases, blood transfusion is an option requiring specialist involvement and strict adherence to safety measures.

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

Beyond supplementation, dietary guidance can help alleviate mild symptoms. Educating patients about appropriate foods is crucial.

For those experiencing menorrhagia alongside IDA, explore potential management strategies.
