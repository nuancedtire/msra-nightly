# Understanding Sickle Cell Anemia

Sickle cell anemia (HbS) is a genetic blood disorder stemming from a single genetic mutation. This mutation replaces glutamine with valine at position 6 in the beta-globin chain, leading to the production of Hemoglobin S (HbS) instead of the normal Hemoglobin A (HbA).

## Genetic Inheritance

Sickle cell disease is inherited in an **autosomal recessive** manner. Offspring of affected individuals can exhibit one of three genetic outcomes:

- **Unaffected (AA):** No symptoms.
- **Heterozygous (AS):** Known as the sickle cell trait, it presents intermediate symptoms and provides some protection against malaria. Approximately 10% of the Afro-Caribbean population in the UK are carriers.
- **Homozygous (SS):** Known as sickle cell anemia, it is the most severe form and manifests clinically around 6 months of age when fetal hemoglobin synthesis ceases.

## Pathophysiology

In individuals with HbSS, red blood cells can become rigid and sickle-shaped due to the polymerization of HbS. This deformation is often triggered by conditions such as hypoxia, cold, dehydration, and acidosis. Sickled cells have a shortened lifespan and can obstruct small blood vessels, leading to ischemia.

## Diagnosis

Adults typically face multiple hospital admissions due to either acute **crises** or long-term complications such as:

- Severe bone pain
- Pulmonary hypertension and chronic lung disease
- Persistent anemia
- Avascular necrosis of bones
- Elevated risk of infections
- Leg ulcers
- Cardiac issues (e.g., cardiomegaly, arrhythmias, myocardial infarctions)
- Neurological issues (e.g., coma, TIAs, strokes)
- Pigmented gallstones, hepatomegaly, nephritis, retinopathies, and spleen atrophy

### Diagnostic Methods

Newborns are screened between 5-9 days old with a heel-prick test. A positive result is confirmed with **hemoglobin electrophoresis** to distinguish between forms of sickle cell syndrome. In older individuals, family history, symptoms, and blood film analysis aid diagnosis.

## Types of Acute Crises

Sickle cell patients may encounter various acute crises:

1. **Thrombotic (Vaso-occlusive):** Painful and often triggered by dehydration, infection, or hypoxia, leading to ischemia.
2. **Aplastic:** Sudden hemoglobin drop due to parvovirus infection.
3. **Hemolytic:** Rare, with an increased hemolysis rate reducing hemoglobin.
4. **Acute Chest Syndrome:** Common post-childhood, presenting with chest pain, low oxygen saturation, and pulmonary infiltrates.
5. **Sequestration:** Blood pooling in organs, causing hypovolemia, collapse, and potentially death.

### Emergency Management

Treatment during a sickle cell crisis includes:

- Pain relief
- Oxygen therapy
- Blood transfusions
- Rehydration
- Antibiotics for infections

## Long-term Management

To manage sickle cell disease and prevent painful episodes, **hydroxyurea** is often prescribed to increase fetal hemoglobin levels. Long-term strategies also recommend vaccinations, including pneumococcal and flu vaccines, to prevent complications like hemolytic anemia secondary to pneumococcal pneumonia.
