# Understanding Prostatitis: Diagnosis and Management

Prostatitis refers to the inflammation of the prostate gland and is categorized into two primary forms: chronic and acute prostatitis. Chronic prostatitis is more prevalent and can affect around 10% of men at some point in their lives, while acute prostatitis is less common but more severe.

## Chronic Prostatitis

Chronic prostatitis is characterized by the persistence of urogenital pain for more than three months. The symptoms can include:

- Pain in the penis, scrotum, perineum, or anus
- Lower urinary tract symptoms like dysuria, frequency, hesitancy, and urgency
- Sexual dysfunction, including erectile dysfunction, painful ejaculation, and decreased libido

Often these symptoms are accompanied by psychological effects such as anxiety and depression. The majority of chronic prostatitis cases are nonbacterial, with only about 10% involving a bacterial infection.

## Acute Prostatitis

Acute prostatitis is a sudden and severe inflammation of the prostate, typically caused by urinary pathogens such as *Escherichia coli*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Klebsiella*, or *Enterococcus*. Symptoms include:

- Severe urinary tract infection symptoms: dysuria and urinary frequency
- Pain around the genital region
- Systemic symptoms: fever, malaise, and chills
- Swollen and tender prostate upon digital rectal examination

Acute prostatitis can be life-threatening and requires immediate medical attention.

## Diagnosis

### Chronic Prostatitis

To diagnose chronic prostatitis, a physician may look for the following:

- Urogenital pain lasting over three months
- Associated lower urinary tract and sexual dysfunction symptoms
- Fluctuating symptom severity over several years

Diagnostic tests can include:

- Urine analysis via dipstick and mid-stream specimen for culture
- Screening for sexually transmitted infections, especially in at-risk individuals
- Urodynamic studies, cystoscopy, and serum Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) testing to rule out other conditions

### Acute Prostatitis

For acute prostatitis, the diagnostic process involves:

- Identifying acute onset symptoms and severe UTI signs
- Digital rectal examination findings
- Urine analysis and infection screening
- Blood cultures and inflammatory markers to assess for sepsis risk

## Management

### Prescribing for Acute Prostatitis

The primary treatment for acute prostatitis includes:

- Antibiotic therapy: oral quinolone or sulphonamide
- Broad-spectrum intravenous antibiotics for patients with signs of sepsis
- Symptomatic relief with analgesia

### Chronic Prostatitis Treatment

For chronic prostatitis, there is no definitive cure, but treatment focuses on symptom management:

- Alpha-blocker medications (e.g., tamsulosin) for urinary symptoms
- Pain control strategies
- Typically, symptoms improve within six months

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

Non-prescription-based approaches may include:

- Temporary catheterization for urinary obstruction
- Surgical intervention for complications like prostatic abscess
- Cognitive behavioral therapy for psychosocial symptoms
- Dietary modifications and pelvic floor muscle training
