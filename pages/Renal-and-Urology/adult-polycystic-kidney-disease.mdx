# Polycystic Kidney Disease: An Overview

## Introduction

Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD) is characterized by the growth of numerous cysts in the kidneys. This genetic disorder manifests in two inheritance patterns: autosomal recessive and autosomal dominant. The recessive form typically leads to a significantly shortened lifespan, with most patients succumbing before the end of childhood. In contrast, the dominant form, also known as adult polycystic disease, has a later onset and patients tend to survive longer, though their life expectancy is still reduced.

## Genetic Background

- **Autosomal Dominant PKD** is more prevalent than its recessive counterpart and is linked to mutations on chromosomes 4 and 16.
  
- **Cyst Development:** Cysts can develop bilaterally in the kidneys and vary in size. They may also emerge in other organs such as the liver, pancreas, and spleen.

- **Characteristics:** The dominant PKD involves progressive dilation and cystic transformation within the renal tubular system, as well as potential implications in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, liver, heart, and central nervous system (CNS).

## Diagnosis of Adult Polycystic Kidney Disease

Patients often exhibit symptoms including:

- Hypertension
- Flank pain
- Renal failure
- Recurrent urinary tract infections (UTIs)
- Renal calculi (kidney stones)

Extrarenal manifestations may include:

- Hepatic cysts, which are the most common extrarenal symptom
- Cerebral berry aneurysms
- Morning headaches, often resulting from hypertension
- Signs indicative of mitral valve prolapse
- Left ventricular hypertrophy
- Diverticular disease
- Abdominal hernias

## Diagnostic Investigations

- Early and accurate diagnosis of PKD can prolong life by delaying the onset of end-stage renal disease.
- **Family History:** A positive family history can suggest PKD as a potential diagnosis.
- **Physical Examination:** Enlarged kidneys that are palpable but non-tender upon examination.
- **Ultrasound:** In adults, it reveals bilaterally enlarged kidneys with multiple cysts, which may also appear in the spleen, liver, or pancreas. For children with a positive family history, minor cystic changes may be evident.
- **Monitoring:** Vital to regularly assess blood pressure and kidney function.
- **Imaging Studies:** An intravenous pyelogram (IVP) may be performed to detect obstructions from cysts in the ureters or renal pelvis.

## Management Strategies

### Prescribing Interventions

- **Blood Pressure Control:** The use of ACE inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers is crucial for managing hypertension and slowing the progression to end-stage renal disease.
- **Infection Management:** Prompt treatment of UTIs can reduce the incidence of renal cyst infection.
- **Disease Progression:** Tolvaptan has been shown to slow the growth of renal cysts and delay the progression to end-stage renal disease.
- **Chemical Avoidance:** Patients should steer clear of substances that could further impair kidney function, including sulphonamides, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and antidiuretic hormone (ADH).

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

- **Hydration:** Increased fluid intake is encouraged to prevent kidney stone formation and slow cyst growth.
- **Advanced Disease Management:** Haemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis is necessary once patients reach end-stage renal failure, with kidney transplantation being the definitive treatment.
- **Ongoing Monitoring:** Regular imaging and laboratory tests are conducted to monitor renal and liver function.
- **Genetic Counselling:** Beneficial for patients, as roughly 50% may require dialysis by age 60.
