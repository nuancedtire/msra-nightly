# Understanding Urinary Tract Infections

## Overview of UTIs

Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs) encompass infections that cause inflammation in the bladder's and urethra's mucosa, known as cystitis and urethritis, respectively. These infections may ascend to the kidneys and ureters, resulting in pyelitis or pyelonephritis.

UTIs are categorized into two types:

- **Uncomplicated UTIs**: Occur in healthy, non-pregnant adult women without urinary tract abnormalities and are not recurrent. These infections typically resolve on their own and infrequently lead to complications like pyelonephritis.
- **Complicated UTIs**: Present in patients with an increased risk of complications. Antibiotic treatment is often necessary in these cases.

## Diagnosis of UTIs

UTIs contribute to 1-3% of consultations in general practice and are a leading reason for empirical antibiotic prescriptions. Escherichia coli (E.coli) is the most common causative organism.

### Symptoms

Patients with UTIs might experience:

- Dysuria (pain during urination)
- Pollakiuria (frequent urination)
- Urinary incontinence
- Pain in the suprapubic region or loins
- Foul-smelling or cloudy urine
- Fever

### Criteria for Diagnosis

A UTI diagnosis requires both typical symptoms and the presence of a significant level of bacteria in the urine (`>105 cfu/ml`). Asymptomatic bacteriuria, except in pregnant women, should not be treated with antibiotics as it doesn't lead to pyelonephritis.

#### Uncomplicated UTI Criteria

To be classified as uncomplicated, a patient must meet the following conditions:

- Healthy and female
- Adult (`>16 years old`)
- Not pregnant
- No urinary tract abnormalities
- No history of recurrent UTIs

#### Complicated UTI Indicators

A UTI is considered complicated if the patient is:

- Male or a child (`<16 years old`)
- Pregnant
- Has comorbidities (e.g., diabetes)
- Has urinary tract abnormalities
- Institutionalized (e.g., nursing home resident)
- Experiencing recurrent UTIs (≥2 episodes within 6 months or >3 episodes within a year)

### Differential Diagnoses

When diagnosing UTIs, consider other conditions like:

- Vaginitis
- Urethral syndrome
- Pyelonephritis
- Overactive bladder (OAB)
- Other infections

## Investigations

- Examine the bladder and kidneys and take a detailed medical history.
- Conduct urine dipstick analysis for nitrite and/or leukocytes.
- Perform urine microscopy to identify leukocytes.
- Urine culture is recommended in specific cases:
  - Prior to antibiotic treatment
  - For high-risk patients
  - When previous empiric antibiotic therapy has failed
  - Always in men
  - *Note*: Uncomplicated UTIs usually don't require urine culture.
- Check for elevated CRP levels, indicative of symptoms like fever and flank pain.
- Administer pregnancy tests for women of childbearing age.

## Management of UTIs

### Prescribing Treatment

For uncomplicated lower UTIs, which are often self-limiting:

- Antibiotics such as Trimethoprim or Nitrofurantoin are used.
- Nitrofurantoin is safe during pregnancy but not at term.
- Monitor for E.coli resistance (10-20%) and adjust antibiotics based on microbiological results.
- Follow local guidelines for antibiotic selection.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Pregnant women with asymptomatic bacteriuria should receive antibiotic treatment to prevent pyelonephritis.
</Callout>

### Non-Prescribing Interventions

For symptomatic relief and prevention:

- Use paracetamol or NSAIDs for pain management.
- Encourage adequate fluid intake to prevent dehydration.
- Educate women on risk factors for recurrent infections, such as changes in sexual partners, frequent intercourse, and spermicide use.
- Cranberry products are not supported by evidence for treating lower UTIs.
