# Testicular Torsion: An Urgent Medical Emergency

Testicular torsion is an acute urological emergency that involves the twisting of a testicle around the spermatic cord, leading to the obstruction of blood flow and potential ischemia. Immediate medical intervention is crucial to prevent irreversible damage to the testicle.

## Understanding Testicular Torsion

### Pathophysiology

- **Venous obstruction**: Initially impedes venous outflow from the testicle.
- **Arterial obstruction**: Following venous congestion, rotations or swelling can obstruct arterial flow, escalating to ischemia and possible necrosis.

### Risk Factors and Demographics

- **Age group**: Primarily affects males under 25 years old.
- **Occurrence**: Can happen spontaneously, during physical exertion, or following trauma.
- **Neonatal considerations**: Extravaginal torsion of the cord and tunica vaginalis can happen due to unsecured tunica vaginalis.
- **Congenital predispositions**: Conditions like Bell Clapper deformity, where the tunica vaginalis attaches higher on the posterior abdominal wall, causing increased testicular mobility within the tunica vaginalis.

## Clinical Presentation

Testicular torsion typically presents with:

- Sudden, severe unilateral scrotal pain.
- Accompanying nausea and vomiting.
- Possible lower abdominal pain.

### Physical Examination Findings

- Swollen, erythematous, and tender scrotum.
- Development of reactive hydrocele.
- High riding or horizontally positioned affected testicle.
- Absent cremasteric reflex, with no pain relief on scrotal elevation.

## Diagnostic Approach

**Urgency Over Investigation**: The critical time window for testicular salvage is typically `<6 hours` from pain onset. Immediate surgical referral should be prioritized over other diagnostic procedures to maximize the chance of salvage, with rates nearing 100% if treated within this timeframe.

### Imaging

- Ultrasound is 93% sensitive and 100% specific for testicular torsion.
- **Note**: Strong clinical suspicion warrants direct urological referral without awaiting imaging results.

## Management Strategies

### Medical Management

- Provide analgesia and antiemetics to alleviate discomfort.
- Manual detorsion and ultrasound doppler may be considered if surgical options are not immediately available.

### Surgical Intervention

- **First-line treatment**: Surgical exploration and detorsion.
- **Procedure**: Utilizes a midline raphe or transverse scrotal incision to access the testicle through an incision in the tunica vaginalis. Detorsion assesses testicular viability.
- **Post-detorsion**: Fixation of salvageable testicles to the scrotal wall to prevent recurrence. Necrotic testicles require removal.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Delay in addressing testicular torsion can lead to loss of the affected testicle, emphasizing the need for rapid medical response.
</Callout>
