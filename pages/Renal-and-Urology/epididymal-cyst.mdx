# Epididymal Cysts and Spermatoceles

Epididymal cysts are non-cancerous, fluid-filled sacs that develop in the head of the epididymis. When these cysts contain sperm, they are specifically called spermatoceles. This condition typically affects men over 40 years of age and can present as multiple cysts, often appearing on both sides.

## Diagnosis of Epididymal Cysts

Epididymal cysts grow slowly and arise due to partial blockage of sperm movement within the vas deferens, leading to cystic expansion of the duct system. Although they are commonly discovered by chance, a patient might notice a non-tender, smooth, soft, and fluctuant lump in the epididymis, which is separate from the testis. These cysts generally remain under 1cm in size.

## Investigations

Epididymal cysts and spermatoceles are typically translucent when examined with a light source. However, other conditions such as testicular cancer, varicocele, hydrocele, and haematocele can also cause chronic scrotal enlargement.

### Ultrasound Imaging

- If transillumination is unsuccessful, performing an ultrasound of the scrotum is recommended to differentiate a spermatocele from a solid mass.

## Management Strategies

### Prescribing Treatment

- For discomfort associated with an epididymal cyst, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) may be prescribed.

### Non-Prescribing Treatment

Small epididymal cysts that do not cause pain are typically not clinically concerning and do not require treatment. However, surgical removal may be necessary if the cyst is large or causes discomfort. It is important to consider the potential complications of surgery, such as:

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Postoperative scarring in the epididymal region can obstruct the ducts and potentially lead to infertility.
</Callout>

#### Alternative Options

- Needle aspiration is an alternative to surgery, offering temporary relief by removing the fluid. Nonetheless, this procedure does not address the root cause, and fluid may quickly reaccumulate.
