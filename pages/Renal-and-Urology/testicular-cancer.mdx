# Understanding Testicular Cancer

Testicular cancer, though a rare entity accounting for merely 1% of all malignancies in males, stands as the most prevalent solid cancer among men aged 15 to 35. It boasts a five-year survival rate upwards of 95%, marking it as one of the most treatable solid neoplasms.

## Types of Testicular Cancer

Testicular cancers fall into two primary groups:

- **Germ Cell Tumours (GCTs)**: These constitute the majority, making up about 95% of testicular cancers.
- **Non-Germ Cell Tumours (NGCTs)**: A smaller category that includes Leydig and Sertoli cell tumours.

For clinical management, GCTs are further classified into:

- Pure seminoma
- Non-seminomatous germ cell tumours (NSGCTs) such as teratomas, embryonal carcinomas, choriocarcinomas, and yolk sac tumours.

## Diagnosis of Testicular Cancer

### Symptoms

Patients may present with various symptoms, including:

- A painless lump or swelling in the testicle
- Heaviness in the scrotum
- Discomfort or pain in the testicle
- Gynaecomastia, which is the development of breast tissue in males
- Metastatic symptoms depending on the tumour type, such as back pain, cough, dyspnoea, or oedema in the lower limbs

### Physical Examination

During examination, a malignant testis often appears firmer than a non-affected testis and less sensitive than one inflamed due to other conditions.

### Differential Diagnosis

It's important to note that many signs and symptoms of testicular cancer could be attributed to non-cancerous conditions such as:

- Epididymo-orchitis
- Torsion
- Hydrocele
- Haematocele
- Epididymal cyst
- Infection

Lymphoma is also considered in the differential diagnosis.

### Risk Factors

Several risk factors increase the likelihood of testicular cancer:

- Cryptorchidism (undescended testicles)
- A family history of testicular cancer
- HIV infection
- Male infertility
- A previous diagnosis of testicular cancer
- Congenital conditions like Klinefelterâ€™s syndrome
- Other factors such as low birthweight, young parental age, multiparity, breech delivery, and tall stature

A significant genetic component is evident, as nearly all tumours exhibit abnormalities on chromosome 12, and carriers of the TGCT1 gene are at a substantially increased risk.

## Investigations

To diagnose testicular cancer, clinicians employ:

- **Tumour Markers**: These include human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG), alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)
- **Imaging**: A bilateral testicular ultrasound scan (ULS), chest X-ray (CXR), and CT scans focused on abdominal lymph nodes and potential lung metastases
- **Histology**: Biopsy taken during orchiectomy used for staging

## Management of Testicular Cancer

### Surgical Intervention

Radical orchidectomy remains the cornerstone of treatment. In cases where fertility preservation is a concern, sperm storage and the option of a testicular prosthesis are offered to patients.

### Prescribing Management

- **Stage I Seminoma**: Surveillance, single-dose adjuvant carboplatin, or adjuvant radiotherapy
- **Metastatic Disease**: Chemotherapy and/or radiotherapy post-radical orchidectomy
- **Advanced Seminoma**: Cisplatin-based chemotherapy for stages III and IV

### Non-Prescribing Management

For stage I NSGCTs, two courses of adjuvant bleomycin, etoposide, and cisplatin (BEP) chemotherapy are recommended following inguinal orchidectomy.
