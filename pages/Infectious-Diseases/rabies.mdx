# Understanding Rabies: Prevention, Diagnosis, and Management

Rabies is a preventable viral disease that affects both humans and animals, primarily transmitted through dog bites. It is more prevalent in regions such as Asia and Africa. Once rabies symptoms manifest, the disease is almost invariably fatal, with a 99-100% fatality rate. The incubation period typically ranges from 1-3 months, but it can be shorter depending on the virus's travel distance to the central nervous system via peripheral nerves. Global vaccination and animal control efforts have significantly lowered rabies incidence. The same vaccine is used for both pre-exposure and post-exposure immunization.

## Diagnosis

Rabies presents in two forms: **furious rabies** and **paralytic rabies**, the latter constituting 20% of human cases.

### Furious Rabies Symptoms

- **Hyperactivity**
- **Hydrophobia** (fear of water), **aerophobia** (fear of fresh air or drafts)
- **Paranoia** and **terror**
- **Hallucinations**
- Death due to cardio-respiratory arrest

### Paralytic Rabies Symptoms

- **Paralysis**
- **Delirium**
- Coma followed by death

## Investigations

The gold standard for diagnosing rabies is the **fluorescent antibody test (FAT)**, an immunohistochemistry procedure. This test uses a detector molecule that binds to the rabies-specific antibody, forming a conjugate. The conjugate then binds to the rabies antigen and can be visualized using fluorescent microscopy.

## Management (Prescribing)

### Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

Immediate post-exposure treatment is crucial to prevent the virus from reaching the central nervous system. Treatment includes:

1. Extensive wound washing for at least ``15 minutes``
2. Immediate administration of the rabies vaccine
3. Rabies immunoglobulin if indicated

The World Health Organization (WHO) categorizes contact with rabid animals and suggests corresponding post-exposure measures:

| Categories of contact with rabid animals | Post-exposure prophylaxis measures                    |
|------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| Category 1 – touching, but no exposure   | Washing only                                          |
| Category 2 – nibbling of uncovered skin  | Washing with immediate vaccination                    |
| Category 3 – transdermal bite            | Washing, immediate vaccination, and rabies immunoglobulin |

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

The most effective prevention method is vaccination. Additional preventive measures include:

- **Vaccination of dogs** against rabies
- **Supervising pets** to prevent exposure
- Avoid handling stray dogs
- Contact animal control if an animal is acting suspiciously
