# Differential Diagnosis of Scrotal Lumps

Scrotal lumps can be indicative of various conditions, and accurate diagnosis is crucial for effective treatment. Herein, we'll discuss the differential diagnosis of scrotal lumps, including key questions to aid in the diagnostic process and the implications of the findings.

## Key Diagnostic Questions

When assessing scrotal lumps, there are four critical questions to consider:

### Can you palpate above the lump?
- If **not**, the lump may represent an *inguinal hernia* or a proximally extending *hydrocele*.

### Is the lump separate from the testis?
- If the lump is **fluid-filled** and separate, it might be an *epididymal cyst*.
- If the lump is **solid** and separate, consider *epididymitis* as a potential cause.
- A **'bag of worms'** feel suggests the presence of a *varicocele*.

### Consistency of the Lump
The physical consistency of the lump provides valuable diagnostic information:
- **Fluid-filled** and attached to the testis: likely a *hydrocele*.
- **Solid** and attached to the testis: differential diagnoses include a *tumour*, *haematocele*, *orchitis*, *gumma*, or *granuloma*.

### Transillumination Test
- Performing a transillumination test can help determine if the lump is fluid-filled, often indicating a *hydrocele* or an *epididymal cyst*.

## Important Considerations

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Always ensure a thorough physical examination and consider further diagnostic imaging or specialist referral in complex or uncertain cases.
</Callout>
