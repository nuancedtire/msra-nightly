# Understanding Dysmenorrhea

Dysmenorrhea, commonly known as painful periods, is a prevalent issue affecting women. This condition is divided into two types: primary and secondary dysmenorrhea, each with distinct characteristics and underlying causes.

## Types of Dysmenorrhea

- **Primary Dysmenorrhea**
  - **Definition**: Recurring, cramp-like, lower abdominal pain aligned with menstruation, absent any discernible disease causing the pain.
  - **Cause(s)**: Prostaglandins released from the endometrium at the onset of menses prompt uterine contractions leading to ischemia and pain.
  - **Clinical Features**:
    - Onset typically occurs in adolescence, within 4-5 years after menarche.
    - Pain in the suprapubic region starts with menstruation, diminishing over 12 to 72 hours; may also be felt in the back or thighs.
    - Accompanying symptoms can include nausea, diarrhea, fatigue, headache, and malaise.
    - Tends to lessen with age.
- **Secondary Dysmenorrhea**
  - **Definition**: Painful menstruation associated with a diagnosable disease.
  - **Clinical Features**:
    - Usually begins later in life, progressively worsens, and improves with the treatment of the underlying condition.

## Diagnosis

Dysmenorrhea is diagnosed through a detailed medical history and a thorough physical examination, including abdominal and pelvic assessments. In young, sexually inactive adolescents with mild cramps, the internal pelvic exam may be deferred. Additional diagnostics such as laboratory tests, imaging, and laparoscopy are employed as needed.

### Primary Dysmenorrhea

A clinical diagnosis made when a woman:

- Experiences recurrent, crampy, midline pelvic pain around the start of menstrual bleeding, lessening over 12 to 72 hours.
- Shows no signs of other disorders that could explain the pain.

### Secondary Dysmenorrhea

Diagnosis includes the same symptoms as primary dysmenorrhea but occurs alongside an identifiable causative disorder. Indicators include signs suggestive of pelvic pathologies like pelvic inflammatory disease, endometriosis, adenomyosis, or fibroids.

### Differential Diagnosis

The recurrent nature of the pain and its timing in relation to menstruation are characteristic of dysmenorrhea. Chronic (>3 months) and non-cyclical pain is categorized as chronic pelvic pain. The diagnosis of primary dysmenorrhea is confirmed once other potential causes have been ruled out.

## Investigations

Tests and procedures are primarily aimed at identifying secondary dysmenorrhea and may include:

- **Pelvic Ultrasound**: Preferably via the transvaginal approach, to check for uterine fibroids, adenomyosis, or endometriosis.
- **Vaginal Swabs**: For detecting chronic pelvic inflammatory disease.
- **Urine Pregnancy Test**: To rule out ectopic pregnancy.
- **Urinalysis**: For identifying urinary tract infections.

## Management

### Prescribing Treatment

The objective of treatment is to alleviate pain sufficiently to allow normal functioning. Therapies for primary dysmenorrhea can be started empirically and are escalated according to symptom severity.

**Non-pharmacological Interventions**:

- Engaging in exercise.
- Applying heat.

**Pharmacological Therapies**:

- **NSAIDs**: First-line treatment, as effective as hormonal contraceptives.
  - Examples: Ibuprofen, Mefenamic acid, Naproxen.
  - If ineffective, switch to another NSAID class or combine with hormonal contraceptives.
- **Hormonal Contraceptives**: Equally effective as NSAIDs; preferred if contraception is also desired.
  - No specific dose or formulation is superior.
  - If ineffective, combine with NSAIDs.

<Callout emoji="ℹ️">
Supportive therapies, though based on limited evidence, are generally safe. Patients may integrate these with other treatments as desired.
</Callout>

### Non-prescribing Management

For persistent dysmenorrhea unresponsive to NSAIDs and hormonal contraception after 3 to 6 cycles, consider:

- Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation.
- Gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonists/antagonists.

These options are limited due to treatment duration, side effects, and cost. Patients who do not respond to these treatments often undergo diagnostic laparoscopy to identify and treat gynecologic diseases like endometriosis.

In cases of dysmenorrhea with heavy menstrual bleeding, endometrial ablation may be considered. Hysterectomy serves as a definitive surgical option for those who have finished childbearing.
