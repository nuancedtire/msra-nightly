# Understanding Herpes Simplex Virus Infections

Herpes simplex virus (HSV), a part of the Alphaherpesviridae subfamily, is characterized by its linear double-stranded DNA structure. HSV undergoes a cycle of initial infection in epithelial cells, latency in neuronal ganglia, and periodic reactivation. While most reactivations do not cause symptoms, they can still lead to virus transmission.

To determine whether an infection is caused by HSV-type 1 (HSV-1) or HSV-type 2 (HSV-2), glycoprotein G-specific serology testing is utilized.

## Clinical Manifestations

HSV-1 and HSV-2 are responsible for various clinical presentations:

- **Ocular, Oral, and Genital Ulcers**: Primary clinical manifestations of both HSV-1 and HSV-2 infections.
- **Neurological, Hepatic, Respiratory, or Ocular Complications**: Less common presentations associated with recurrent or primary HSV infections.
- **Transmission**: HSV is predominantly transmitted through body fluids, such as during sexual intercourse.

### Genital Herpes

Both HSV-1 and HSV-2 can lead to genital herpes, which may present as:

- **Primary Symptoms in Women**: Fever, neuralgia, and painful genital ulcers may be accompanied by discharge and dysuria. Lesions can appear on the vulva, perineum, cervix, vagina, and buttocks.
- **Primary Symptoms in Men**: Typically milder, with urethritis and vesicles on the glans or shaft of the penis.

**Asymptomatic Shedding**: HSV can be shed from the skin even without visible lesions, potentially leading to transmission.

## Diagnosis

Consider the following diagnostic factors:

- Presence of risk factors:
  - Use of immunosuppressive medication
  - High-risk sexual behavior (multiple sexual partners, lack of condom use)
  - HIV positive status
  - Female gender and/or non-Caucasian race
  - Increased age
- Clinical signs:
  - Genital ulcers or vesicles
  - Dysuria in women
  - Lymphadenopathy
  - Oral ulcers
  - Fever

## Investigations

The following tests are used to diagnose HSV:

- **Viral Culture**: Identifies the presence of HSV when lesions are evident.
- **HSV Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**:
  - Measures viral load in copies per millilitre
  - Offers higher sensitivity than viral culture; involves swab placement in PCR buffer
- **Glycoprotein G-based Type-Specific Serology**: Determines if antibodies to HSV-1 or HSV-2 are present.

## Management (Prescribing)

The primary approach to managing HSV infections includes:

- **First-line Treatments**: Oral acyclovir, valaciclovir, or famciclovir.
- **Adjunct Symptomatic Treatment**: Provided alongside primary medications.
- **Second-line Treatment for Oral Infection**: Topical antivirals.
- **Immunocompromised Patients**:
  - Foscarnet as third-line medication for oral infections.
  - Foscarnet as a second-line treatment for genital infections.

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

Education is crucial for:

- **High-risk Individuals**: To reduce infection chances and prevent transmission.
- **HSV Positive Patients**: To recognize symptoms and manage the condition.
