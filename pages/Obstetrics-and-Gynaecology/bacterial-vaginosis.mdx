# Bacterial Vaginosis Overview

Bacterial vaginosis (BV) is a prevalent vaginal condition in women, particularly among those who are sexually active. While not classified as a sexually transmitted infection (STI), sexual intercourse is a primary risk factor. The condition arises from an imbalance in the vaginal flora, specifically the overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria like Gardnerella vaginalis, which suppresses the lactic acid-producing aerobic lactobacilli. This imbalance leads to an increase in vaginal pH.

## Risk Factors

Several factors can predispose women to bacterial vaginosis, including:

- Use of intrauterine devices (IUDs)
- Douching practices
- Being pregnant

For expectant mothers, BV is associated with heightened risks of premature delivery, babies with low birth weight, and chorioamnionitis.

## Diagnosis and Symptoms

### Symptoms

Although 50% of individuals with BV may not exhibit symptoms, those who do may experience:

- A fishy or offensive vaginal discharge
- A burning sensation during urination
- Possible mild itching, typically without vaginal redness

### Diagnostic Criteria

Bacterial vaginosis is diagnosed using Amselâ€™s criteria, requiring at least three of the following:

- Thin, white or grey vaginal discharge
- Positive whiff test, where the addition of potassium hydroxide produces a fishy odor
- Vaginal pH greater than `4.5`
- Presence of clue cells on microscopic examination

## Management Strategies

### Treatment

The primary treatment for BV involves anti-anaerobic antibiotics, with options including:

- Oral metronidazole for 5-7 days, or as a topical application
- Clindamycin as an alternative therapy

Post-treatment, 70-80% of cases are resolved, yet approximately 50% may experience a relapse within three months.
