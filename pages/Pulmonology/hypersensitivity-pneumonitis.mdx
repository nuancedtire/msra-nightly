# Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis: An Overview

Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP), formerly known as extrinsic allergic alveolitis, is a complex interstitial lung disease characterized by pulmonary inflammation due to hypersensitivity to inhaled organic substances. A crucial aspect of managing HP lies in avoiding allergens.

## Understanding Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis

HP results from an allergic reaction to inhaled organic antigens, causing inflammation of the alveoli and surrounding lung tissue. This condition is manageable with the right approach, primarily through the avoidance of allergens.

### Diagnosis Challenges

The absence of a definitive diagnostic test makes identifying HP difficult. Common signs and symptoms are broad and include:

- Dry cough
- Progressive difficulty breathing
- Systemic symptoms such as weight loss, fever, and myalgia
- Inspiratory crackles and variable squeaks upon lung auscultation

A wide array of antigens can trigger HP, with each type having a specific name. In the UK, for example, "Farmer's lung," caused by exposure to moldy straw, is a prevalent form of HP.

HP can also be categorized based on the clinical course into two forms:

- Acute HP
- Chronic HP

### Investigation Findings

To aid in the diagnosis of HP, various investigations and their corresponding findings are considered:

- **_Imaging:_** Diffuse ground-glass opacities on chest X-ray and high-resolution CT scans
- **_Pulmonary Function Tests:_** Indication of a restrictive lung disease pattern
- **_Blood Tests:_** Presence of neutrophilia and elevated inflammatory markers
- **_Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL):_** Lymphocytosis
- **_Lung Biopsy:_** Lymphocytic inflammation

## Non-Prescribing Management

The prognosis for HP is generally favorable when complete allergen avoidance is possible. When total avoidance is unfeasible, measures should be taken to minimize exposure:

- Enhance ventilation
- Utilize positive pressure face masks
