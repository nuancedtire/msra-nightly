# Understanding Pneumothorax

Pneumothorax refers to the presence of air in the pleural space, which can be classified into **primary**, **secondary**, or **traumatic** types.

## Types of Pneumothorax

### Primary Spontaneous Pneumothorax (PSP)

- Occurs without an external event and in the absence of underlying lung disease.
- Often results from the rupture of unrecognized subpleural blebs.

### Secondary Spontaneous Pneumothorax (SSP)

- Develops due to complications from existing lung diseases.
- Common causes include:
  - Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)
  - Tuberculosis (in endemic regions)
  - Cystic fibrosis
  - Lung malignancies
  - Pneumonia

### Traumatic Pneumothorax

- Results from penetrating or non-penetrating trauma.
- **Iatrogenic Pneumothorax**: Caused by medical procedures, especially central venous catheterization.

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Presentation

- **Primary Pneumothorax**: Typically occurs in young males with sudden onset dyspnea and pleuritic chest pain.
- **Secondary Pneumothorax**: Often seen in older patients with existing lung diseases like COPD.

### Physical Examination

- Minimal findings in small pneumothorax.
- In larger pneumothorax:
  - Reduced chest expansion on the affected side
  - Hyper-expansion of the affected side
  - Diminished breath sounds
  - Absent tactile or vocal fremitus
  - Hyperresonant percussion

## Investigations

- **Chest X-ray**: First-line investigation; look for the visceral pleural line and asymmetrical lung markings.
- **CT Chest and Ultrasound**: Used when x-ray diagnosis is unclear.
- **CT Scanning**: Best for measuring pneumothorax size. `Small: <2 cm`, `Large: ≥2 cm` from chest wall to visceral line at the hilum.
- **Arterial Blood Gas**: Often abnormal in pneumothorax, e.g., `SpO2 <92%`.

## Emergency Management

A tension pneumothorax is a critical condition where air pressure in the pleural space disrupts venous return, leading to:

- Hypotension
- Tachycardia
- Severe dyspnea

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** This is a medical emergency requiring immediate decompression of the affected hemithorax. Do not wait for a chest x-ray.
</Callout>

### Indications and Techniques

- **Tracheal Deviation**: A late sign, not always indicative.
- **Decompression**: According to ATLS, insert a cannula into the `4th or 5th intercostal space` in the mid-axillary line. Resuscitation Council guidelines accept both this and the `2nd intercostal space` in the mid-clavicular line.
- Expect an immediate rush of air, confirming the conversion to a simple pneumothorax.

## Management Strategies

### Prescribing Management

- Observe clinically stable patients with small PSP and provide high-flow oxygen (`10 L/min`), which accelerates reabsorption.

### Non-Prescribing Management

![Pneumothorax Management](https://medibuddy.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Pneumothorax-2023-214x300.gif)

#### Primary Pneumothorax

- **Small Pneumothorax**: If `rim of air <2cm` and patient is well, consider discharge without further treatment.
- **Large Pneumothorax**: Perform percutaneous needle aspiration. If unsuccessful, repeat or insert a chest drain.

#### Secondary Pneumothorax

- **Aspiration**: For patients `<50 years` or with small pneumothorax (`<2cm` on x-ray).
- **Chest Drain**: For others, followed by 24-hour observation before discharge.
