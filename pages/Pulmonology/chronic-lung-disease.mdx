# Pulmonary Fibrosis: Upper and Lower Zone Patterns

## Introduction

Pulmonary fibrosis is a respiratory condition characterized by the progressive scarring of lung tissue, affecting the upper and lower zones of the lungs differently. Understanding the distribution patterns can assist healthcare professionals in diagnosing the underlying causes of fibrosis. This article outlines the various conditions associated with fibrosis in these distinct lung zones.

## Upper Zone Fibrosis

The following conditions are typically associated with fibrosis that predominantly affects the upper zones of the lungs:

- **Sarcoidosis**: A disease characterized by the growth of tiny collections of inflammatory cells in different parts of the body, especially the lungs.
- **Pulmonary Tuberculosis**: An infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, primarily affecting the lungs.
- **Silicosis**: A lung disease caused by inhaling tiny bits of silica, commonly found in sand, rock, and mineral ores.
- **Coal Workers’ Pneumoconiosis**: Also known as "black lung disease," it results from long-term exposure to coal dust.
- **Cystic Fibrosis**: A genetic disorder that causes severe damage to the lungs and digestive system.
- **Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis**: An allergic reaction in the lungs due to inhaling certain dusts to which the individual has become sensitized.
- **Pneumocystis Pneumonia**: A form of pneumonia caused by the fungus Pneumocystis jirovecii, commonly affecting people with weakened immune systems.
- **Ankylosing Spondylitis**: An inflammatory disease that can cause some of the vertebrae in the spine to fuse together, affecting the lungs in some cases.

## Lower Zone Fibrosis

Conditions that are more likely to cause fibrosis in the lower zones of the lungs include:

- **Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis**: A chronic, progressive lung disease with no known cause.
- **Connective Tissue Disease**: Except for ankylosing spondylitis, these autoimmune disorders can also affect the lower lung zones.
- **Asbestosis**: A lung disease resulting from the inhalation of asbestos fibers, leading to long-term lung damage.
- **Medication-induced**: Certain medications like methotrexate, nitrofurantoin, and amiodarone may cause pulmonary fibrosis as a side effect.

<Callout emoji="ℹ️">
  **Note:** The distribution of fibrosis in the lungs can provide valuable clues to the underlying etiology of the disease. A thorough patient history and diagnostic workup are essential for accurate diagnosis and treatment.
</Callout>
