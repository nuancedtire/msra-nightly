# Cardiac Tamponade: Diagnosis and Management

Cardiac tamponade is a critical medical condition caused by the accumulation of substances like blood, fluid, pus, clots, or gas in the pericardial space, which lies between the heart and its fibrous casing, the pericardial sac. This build-up exerts pressure on the heart, reducing ventricular filling and potentially leading to life-threatening complications.

## Diagnosis

Cardiac tamponade is a rare condition, with an incidence of approximately `2 cases per 10,000` in the US population. It is crucial not to overlook it, especially since its causes and frequency can vary by age group. Younger individuals may experience tamponade due to trauma or HIV, whereas older adults might encounter it more often due to malignancies or chronic kidney disease.

### Causes

- **Pericarditis:**
  - Associated with malignancies, especially proximal tumors like breast or lung cancer
  - Acute myocardial infarction and post-infarction pericarditis (Dressler’s syndrome)
  - Infections: viral (HIV), bacterial (tuberculosis), fungal
  - Connective tissue diseases: Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE), rheumatoid arthritis, dermatomyositis, systemic sclerosis
  - Radiotherapy
  - Myxoedema (hypothyroidism)
  - Chronic kidney disease
  - Post-operative hemorrhagic pericarditis
  - Idiopathic origins
- **Drugs:** Hydralazine, isoniazid, minoxidil
- **Diagnostic procedures** with cardiac perforation
- **Trauma:** Incidence of `2%` in penetrating trauma, rare in blunt trauma
- **Surgery**
- **Aortic dissection**

### Symptoms

- Subacute presentations may include:
  - Anxiety
  - Fatigue
  - Edema
  - Confusion
- Other symptoms:
  - Dyspnea, tachycardia, tachypnea
  - Cold extremities due to compromised perfusion
  - Possible association with pericarditis features

### Signs

- **Beck's Triad** (late sign, rarely observed together):
  - Hypotension
  - Muffled heart sounds
  - Distended neck veins
- Additional signs:
  - Tachycardia, tachypnea, hepatomegaly
  - Possible pericardial rub
  - **Pulsus paradoxus:** A decrease in systemic blood pressure during inspiration of more than `10mmHg` differentiates patients with pericardial effusion, with or without tamponade
  - **Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP):** `Y descent` abolished due to increased intrapericardial pressure preventing diastolic filling of ventricles
  - Cyanosis and pulmonary edema

## Diagnosis and Management of Emergencies

- **Blood Tests:**
  - Elevated creatine kinase (in cardiac trauma)
  - Renal function analysis
  - Full Blood Count (FBC) for infection
  - Antinuclear antibody, ESR, rheumatoid factors for connective tissue disorders
  - HIV and Acid-Fast Bacilli testing
- **Electrocardiogram (ECG):** May reveal sinus tachycardia, low voltage QRS complexes, PR depression
- **Chest X-Ray:** Potential findings include cardiomegaly, 'bottle-shaped' heart, pericardial calcifications, evidence of trauma (e.g., broken ribs)
- **Transthoracic Echocardiogram:** May show reduced ventricular filling
- **Pericardiocentesis:** Aspirate sent for culture and cytology

## Management

Patients should be relocated to an intensive care unit. Echocardiography-guided pericardiocentesis is the preferred treatment for fluid removal from the pericardial space. However, in certain cases, this procedure might be hazardous or insufficient to alleviate symptoms. If caused by trauma, thoracotomy might be necessary to stop bleeding. Addressing the underlying cause is crucial.

### Primary Survey: Airway, Breathing, Circulation

- Administer oxygen
- Provide small volumes of fluid to maintain intravascular volume; use blood if trauma-related
- Enhance venous return through leg elevation
- Utilize positive inotropic drugs, e.g., Dobutamine
- Avoid positive-pressure mechanical ventilation to maintain venous return

### Surgical Management

- **Pericardiodesis:** Used for recurrent pericardial effusion or tamponade. Drugs such as corticosteroids, tetracycline, or antineoplastic drugs are administered via an intrapericardial catheter.
- **Pericardio-peritoneal shunt:** Helps prevent recurrent tamponade in patients with malignant pericardial effusions.
- **Pericardiectomy:** Resection of the pericardium, rarely required.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Always monitor vital signs continuously during the procedure.
</Callout>
