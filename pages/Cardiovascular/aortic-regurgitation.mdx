# Understanding Aortic Regurgitation

Aortic regurgitation is a condition characterized by a malfunctioning aortic valve, which leads to the backflow of blood from the aorta into the left ventricle during diastole. This condition typically arises from structural issues affecting either the aortic valve or the aortic root. The left ventricle compensates by enlarging to maintain adequate cardiac output.

## Diagnosis

### Causes

Aortic regurgitation can result from various underlying causes, including:

- **Bicuspid aortic valve:** A congenital defect where the valve has two cusps instead of three.
- **Rheumatic fever:** An abnormal autoimmune response often following a streptococcal throat infection.
- **Infective endocarditis:** Bacterial growths on cardiac valves hinder their proper functioning.
- **Collagen valvular diseases**
- **Degenerative aortic valve disease**

Individuals with a history of specific conditions have an increased risk, including:

- Aortic dissection
- Systemic lupus erythematosus
- Marfan’s syndrome
- Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
- Turner syndrome
- Ankylosing spondylitis
- Reactive arthritis
- Takayasu’s disease
- Behcet’s disease

*Note:* Rheumatic heart disease is the most prevalent cause worldwide. In developed regions, congenital and degenerative abnormalities are more common. Aortic regurgitation typically presents between the ages of 40-60, with severe cases being rare.

### Symptoms and Signs

**Common symptoms** include fatigue, shortness of breath, peripheral edema, and anginal chest pain.

**Clinical signs** may involve:

- **Soft S1** heart sound
- **Diastolic murmur:** Best heard in the aortic area with the patient sitting forward and exhaling. Not well transmitted to the carotids.
- **Exaggerated arterial pulsations**
- **Low diastolic pressure** and **wide pulse pressure**
- **Corrigan pulse:** A "water-hammer" pulse, head bobbing with each pulse, and pulses bisferiens, though rare.
- **Complete valve collapse** may produce a 'cooing dove' sound, necessitating urgent valve replacement.

## Investigations

- **Full 12-lead ECG**
- **Chest X-ray:** To check for heart failure signs.
- **Echocardiography:** Essential for assessing the severity of regurgitation by determining `regurgitant volume` or `fraction`.
- **Cardiac magnetic resonance (CMR):** Useful in conditions like Marfan’s syndrome or when an echocardiogram indicates aortic enlargement or a bicuspid aortic valve.
- **Cardiac catheterization:** Evaluates cardiac anatomy before valvular surgery.

## Management

### Pharmacologic Treatment

- **Vasodilators and inotropes** can be used short-term for severe heart failure preceding aortic valve surgery.
- For chronic severe aortic regurgitation with heart failure, **ACE inhibitors** or **angiotensin-II antagonists** are recommended, especially if surgery is contraindicated or if left ventricular dysfunction persists post-surgery.
- In Marfan’s syndrome, **beta-blockers** may help slow aortic root dilatation, reducing complication risks.

### Non-Pharmacologic Management

- Patients with mild to moderate aortic regurgitation should be reviewed annually, with echocardiography every two years.
- Follow-ups can increase to six-month intervals if significant changes in ejection fraction or ventricular diameter occur.
- Symptomatic and asymptomatic patients with deteriorating ventricular function should undergo valve replacement, although experienced centers increasingly perform valve repairs.
- **Valve-sparing aortic replacement** is an option for combined aortic root dilatation and valve regurgitation.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Surgery-related mortality is low (`1-4%`), but risk increases with age and reduced left ventricular function.
</Callout>
