# Aortic Regurgitation

Aortic regurgitation is a condition characterized by an **insufficient** or **leaky** aortic valve, allowing backflow of blood from the aorta into the left ventricle during diastole. This results in left ventricular dilation as the heart attempts to maintain adequate cardiac output.

## Causes

Aortic regurgitation may be caused by several factors, including:

- **Bicuspid aortic valve** (congenital)
- **Rheumatic fever** (abnormal autoimmune response most commonly following streptococcal throat infection)
- **Infective endocarditis** (bacterial vegetations affecting cardiac valves)
- **Collagen valvular diseases**
- **Degenerative aortic valve disease**

### Risk Factors

Patients with a history of the following conditions may be at increased risk:

- Aortic dissection
- Systemic lupus erythematosus
- Marfan syndrome
- Ehlers-Danlos syndrome
- Turner syndrome
- Ankylosing spondylitis
- Reactive arthritis
- Takayasu's disease
- Behcet’s disease

**Rheumatic heart disease** is the most prevalent cause of aortic regurgitation globally. In developed regions, congenital and degenerative conditions are more common. The **peak age of presentation** is between **40-60 years**, and severe cases are relatively rare.

## Symptoms

Symptomatic aortic regurgitation may present with:

- **Fatigue**
- **Shortness of breath**
- **Peripheral edema**
- **Anginal chest pain**

### Signs

Examination findings may include:

- Soft S1 heart sound
- Diastolic murmur best heard in the aortic area (patient seated forward and in expiration)
- Exaggerated arterial pulsations
- Low diastolic pressure and wide pulse pressure
- *Corrigan pulse* (also known as 'water hammer' pulse) and head bobbing are rarely observed in the UK due to infrequent severe cases.
- **Complete aortic valve collapse** can produce a sound akin to 'a cooing dove', necessitating urgent valve replacement.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Immediate evaluation and treatment are crucial in cases of complete valve collapse.
</Callout>

## Investigations

The following investigations can assist in diagnosing aortic regurgitation:

- Full **12 lead ECG**
- **Chest X-ray**: may reveal signs of heart failure
- **Echocardiography**: critical for quantifying severity by assessing the ‘regurgitant volume’ or fraction
- **Cardiac magnetic resonance (CMR)**: indicated in Marfan syndrome or detected by echocardiography for aortic enlargement or a bicuspid aortic valve
- **Cardiac catheterization**: to evaluate cardiac anatomy before valvular surgery

## Management (Prescribing)

For **short-term treatment** of severe heart failure prior to aortic valve surgery, consider: 

- **Vasodilators**
- **Inotropes**

In cases of chronic severe aortic regurgitation with heart failure:

- **ACE inhibitors** or **angiotensin-II antagonists** are used for managing hypertension if surgery is contraindicated or left ventricular dysfunction persists postoperatively.

In patients with **Marfan syndrome**, beta-blockers may help slow aortic root dilatation, thereby reducing the risk of complications.

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

For patients with mild to moderate aortic regurgitation:

- Review annually, with echocardiography every two years.
- Increase monitoring to six-monthly if significant changes in ejection fraction or ventricular diameter are noted.

### Surgical Considerations

- Symptomatic patients, as well as asymptomatic patients with deteriorating ventricular dysfunction, should undergo **valve replacement**. Valve repairs are increasingly performed at experienced centers.
- **Valve-sparing aortic replacement** is an option for treating combined aortic root dilation and valve regurgitation.
- **Mortality** rates for these surgeries are low (1-4%), though they increase with age and declining left ventricular function.
