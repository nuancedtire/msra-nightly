# Subclavian Steal Syndrome

## Overview

Subclavian steal syndrome is a vascular condition caused by a proximal stenosing lesion or occlusion in the subclavian artery, leading to **syncope** or **neurological deficits**. It typically occurs in response to increased blood flow demand in the affected arm, resulting in reversed blood flow in the ipsilateral vertebral artery.

<details>
<summary>Pathophysiology</summary>

- When the arm requires more oxygenated blood (e.g., during exercise), the stenosis or occlusion diverts blood flow.
- Chronically, this diversion leads to a decrease in blood flow to the posterior circulation (brain).
- **Collaterals** from the contralateral vertebral artery often compensate for the reduced flow.

</details>

## Clinical Features

### Upper Limb Symptoms
- **Arm Claudication**: Pain or discomfort in the arm with exertion.
- **Weakness**: Decreased strength in the affected arm.

### Neurological Features
- Less common but may include:
  - **Dizziness**
  - **Visual disturbances**
  - **Syncope**

<details>
<summary>Common Signs and Symptoms</summary>

- **Chest Pain**
  - Crushing or pressure-like
  - May radiate to the left arm, jaw, or back
  - Often accompanied by sweating

- **Respiratory**
  - Shortness of breath
  - Rapid breathing
  - Orthopnea

- **Other Symptoms**
  - Nausea and vomiting
  - Fatigue
  - Light-headedness
  - Anxiety

</details>

## Risk Factors

**Risk factors associated with subclavian steal syndrome mirror those of atherosclerosis and include:**

- **Hypertension**
- **Diabetes mellitus**
- **Smoking**
- **Older age**

## Investigations

1. **Initial Investigation**
   - **Duplex Ultrasound (US)**: Shows retrograde flow in the affected vertebral artery during exercise.

2. **Definitive Investigation**
   - **CT or MR Angiography**: Identifies the occlusive lesion and assesses cerebral vasculature.
     - *Statistics*: 80% of patients with this syndrome have extra cranial vascular disease.

3. **Other Imaging**
   - **Chest Radiograph**: Evaluates for external compression on the subclavian artery.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Early diagnosis is crucial as it may prevent significant neurological deficits.
</Callout>

## Treatment

**Management strategies include:**

- **Cardiovascular Risk Factor Modification**
  - Control of blood pressure, glucose levels, and smoking cessation.

- **Surgical Intervention**
  - **Endovascular Techniques**: Stenting or angioplasty to open occluded vessels.
  - **Bypass Surgery**: Redirecting blood flow around the occluded area.
