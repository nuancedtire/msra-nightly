# Fallot’s Tetralogy

Fallot’s Tetralogy is a congenital heart defect characterized by **four main features**:

1. **Pulmonary Stenosis**
2. **Right Ventricular Hypertrophy**
3. **Overriding Aorta**
4. **Ventricular Septal Defect**

It is recognized as the most common cause of cyanotic congenital heart disease and typically presents between **1-2 months of age** with episodes of cyanosis that can lead to syncope and seizures if severe.

## Clinical Presentation

<details>
<summary>Common Signs and Symptoms</summary>

- **Cyanosis**
  - Bluish discoloration of skin, lips, and nails
  - Worsens with crying or exertion
- **Syncope**
  - Episodes of fainting
  - Often triggered by physical activity
- **Clubbing**
  - Enlargement of distal phalanges over time
- **Heart Murmur**
  - Due to right ventricular outflow tract obstruction

</details>

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis of Fallot's Tetralogy may involve:

- **Echocardiogram**: Essential for assessing the anatomy of the heart.
- **Chest X-Ray**: Can show a "boot-shaped" heart due to hypertrophy.
- **Electrocardiogram (ECG)**: Often shows right ventricular hypertrophy.

## Management

Management of Fallot’s Tetralogy may include:

- **Surgical Intervention**:
  - Complete repair is often performed within the first year of life.
  - Procedures may include patching the ventricular septal defect and relieving pulmonary stenosis.
  
- **Medical Management**:
  - **Prostaglandins** can be administered to maintain patent ductus arteriosus in critically ill infants.

## Prognosis

- With early diagnosis and treatment, many children have good outcomes and can lead normal lives.
  
  <Callout emoji="⚠️">
  **Warning:** Delayed treatment can lead to severe complications, including heart failure and sudden cardiac death.
  </Callout>
