# Complications of Myocardial Infarction

A myocardial infarction is a major physiological event, leading to both cardiac and non-cardiac sequelae in patients. Awareness of these complications is crucial for effective **diagnosis**, **investigation**, and **management**.

## Cardiac Complications

### Pericarditis

- **Incidence:** Occurs in approximately `10%` of patients, most commonly between `24` and `96 hours` following an anterior infarction.
- **Reduced risk** with early reperfusion during acute infarction.

**Presentation:**
- Low-grade fever
- Pericardial rub
- Pleuritic chest pain

**Investigations:**
- ECG: ST elevation in all leads with NO reciprocal ST depression.
- Chest X-Ray: May show globular cardiac enlargement.
- Echocardiography: May reveal a small pericardial effusion.

**Management:**
- Anti-inflammatory drugs and analgesia
- Repeat echocardiogram if an effusion was initially present.

### Dressler’s Syndrome

- **Incidence:** Occurs in `1%` to `3%` of patients post-myocardial infarction.
- **Timing:** Presents as pericarditis `2-5 weeks` after MI.

**Symptoms:**
- Self-limiting febrile illness
- Pleuritic or pericardial pain
- Possible pleural and pericardial effusions

**Management:**
- Initial treatment with NSAIDs
- Steroids may be considered in severe cases or when repeated effusion drainage is needed.

### Reocclusion of the Artery

- **Occurrence:** Affects a minority of patients overall, particularly those post-fibrinolytic therapy.
- **Notable risk:** Higher incidence in patients with diabetes mellitus or prior myocardial infarctions.

**Management:** Follow the ACS pathway.

### Angina

- **Timing:** Possible `a few hours` to `30 days` following myocardial infarction.
- **Common in:** Patients with NSTEMI and those treated with fibrinolytics versus PCI.

### Heart Failure

- **Prognosis:** Often a poor prognostic feature; pulmonary edema is relatively common.
- **Causes:** Myocardial damage, arrhythmias, or mechanical complications (e.g., mitral regurgitation, ventricular septal defect).

### Ventricular Septal Rupture / Free Wall Rupture

**Timing:** Usually presents `2-7 days` post-infarction.

- **Mortality Rate:** >`90%`.
- **Risk Factors:**
  - Increasing age
  - Female gender
  - Non-smoker
  - Anterior infarction
  - Higher heart rate on admission
  - First myocardial infarction
  - Hypertension

**Ventricular Septal Rupture:**
- **Signs:** New harsh pansystolic murmur best heard at the left lower sternal border, worsening hemodynamic instability, and biventricular failure.
- **Diagnosis:** Transoesophageal echocardiography.
- **Management:** Urgent surgical repair.

**Free Wall Rupture:**
- **Consequences:** Causes bleeding into the pericardium, leading to cardiac tamponade and progressively poorer cardiac function.
- **Management:** Emergency pericardiocentesis and cardiac surgery are critical.

### Acute Mitral Regurgitation

- **Common with:** Infero-posterior infarctions.
- **Causes:** Ischemia, necrosis, or rupture of the papillary muscle.
- **Prognosis:** Predicts poor prognosis but is often transient and asymptomatic.

## Non-Cardiac Complications

### Depression

- **Prevalence:** Significant depression affects about `20%` of patients post-myocardial infarction.
- **Risk:** Myocardial infarction increases the risk of suicide in the following period.

**Management:**
- Referral for talking therapies or appropriate antidepressant therapy as indicated.

## Management Strategies

### Management (Prescribing)

- **Antiplatelet agent:** Long-term low-dose aspirin plus clopidogrel or ticagrelor.
- **Beta-blockers:** Shown to reduce mortality; should be started within hours of infarction.
- **ACE inhibitors**
- **Cholesterol-lowering agents:** Statins.
- **Aldosterone antagonist:** Eplerenone for patients with severe left ventricular dysfunction unless contraindicated due to renal impairment or hyperkalemia.

### Management (Non-Prescribing)

#### Driving

<details>
<summary>Guidelines for Driving Post-MI</summary>

- **Ordinary driving/motorcycle license:** No need to notify the DVLA if successfully treated by coronary angioplasty; can drive after:
  - No other urgent revascularization planned within `four weeks`.
  - Left ventricular ejection fraction is ≥`40%` prior to discharge.
  - No other disqualifying condition.

- **If not successfully treated by coronary angioplasty:** Can drive after `four weeks`.

- **Vocational drivers of large goods/passenger-carrying vehicles:**
  - All acute coronary syndromes disqualify the license holder from driving for at least `six weeks`.
  - DVLA must be notified.
</details>

#### Employment

- **Return to Work:** Should not typically be delayed beyond `three months` to optimize chances of successful return. 
- **Special cases:** Patients who have suffered a cardiac arrest or undergone CABG may require up to `six months` off work.
