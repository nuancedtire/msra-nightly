# Complications Following Myocardial Infarction

A myocardial infarction is a significant physiological event that can lead to both cardiac and non-cardiac complications. As a healthcare provider, it is crucial to recognize these complications to effectively diagnose, investigate, and manage them.

## Cardiac Complications

### Pericarditis

Pericarditis typically occurs between 24 and 96 hours after an anterior myocardial infarction, affecting approximately 10% of patients. The incidence decreases with early reperfusion treatment. It presents with:

- Low-grade fever
- Pericardial rub
- Pleuritic chest pain

ECG findings include `ST` elevation in all leads without reciprocal `ST` depression. A chest X-ray may show globular cardiac enlargement, and echocardiography might reveal a small pericardial effusion. Treatment involves anti-inflammatory drugs and analgesia, with a repeat echocardiogram if an effusion is detected.

### Dressler's Syndrome

Dressler's syndrome appears in 1-3% of patients 2-5 weeks post-myocardial infarction. It manifests similarly to pericarditis and is less common with early reperfusion therapy. Symptoms include a self-limiting febrile illness, pericardial or pleural pain, and potential pleural and pericardial effusions. The cause is possibly autoimmune. Management starts with NSAIDs, but steroids are used for severe cases or recurrent effusion drainage.

### Reocclusion of the Artery

This occurs in a minority of patients but is more prevalent following fibrinolytic therapy, especially in those with diabetes mellitus or previous myocardial infarction. These patients often have poorer outcomes. Management follows the ACS pathway.

### Angina

Angina can occur between a few hours and 30 days post-myocardial infarction, particularly in patients with NSTEMI or those treated with fibrinolytics compared to PCI.

### Heart Failure

Heart failure is a poor prognostic indicator, often presenting as pulmonary edema after myocardial infarction. It usually results from myocardial damage but can also be caused by arrhythmias or mechanical complications such as mitral regurgitation or ventricular septal defect.

### Ventricular Septal Rupture and Free Wall Rupture

These complications typically present 2-7 days after infarction, with a mortality rate exceeding 90%. Risk factors include:

- Increasing age
- Female gender
- Non-smoker status (though smoking is a major risk factor for infarction)
- Anterior infarction
- Increased heart rate on admission
- First myocardial infarction
- Hypertension

**Ventricular Septal Rupture**: Signs include a new harsh pansystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border, worsening hemodynamic instability, and biventricular failure. Diagnosis is via transesophageal echocardiography, requiring urgent surgical repair.

**Free Wall Rupture**: This leads to bleeding into the pericardium, causing cardiac tamponade and deteriorating cardiac function, often resulting in immediate death. Emergency pericardiocentesis and cardiac surgery are crucial.

### Acute Mitral Regurgitation

Common in infero-posterior infarctions, this condition may result from ischemia, necrosis, or papillary muscle rupture. Although it predicts a poor prognosis, it is often transient and asymptomatic.

## Non-Cardiac Complications

### Depression

Approximately 20% of patients experience significant depression post-myocardial infarction, increasing the risk of suicide. Treatment includes referral for talking therapies or antidepressants as necessary.

## Management

### Prescribing

- **Antiplatelet Agents**: Long-term low-dose aspirin with clopidogrel or ticagrelor.
- **Beta-blockers**: Reduce mortality and should be initiated within hours of infarction.
- **ACE Inhibitors**
- **Statins**: For cholesterol reduction.
- **Aldosterone Antagonist**: Eplerenone for patients with severe left ventricular dysfunction, unless contraindicated by renal impairment or hyperkalemia.

### Non-Prescribing

#### Driving

- **Ordinary License**: No need to notify the DVLA. Driving may resume one week after successful coronary angioplasty if:
  - No urgent revascularization is planned within four weeks.
  - Left ventricular ejection fraction is at least `40%` before discharge.
  - No other disqualifying conditions exist.

If not treated by angioplasty, driving can resume after four weeks.

- **Vocational Drivers**: All acute coronary syndromes disqualify drivers for at least six weeks. Notification to the DVLA is required.

#### Employment

Returning to work should not be delayed beyond three months to increase the likelihood of a successful return. Patients with cardiac arrest or CABG may need up to six months off work.
