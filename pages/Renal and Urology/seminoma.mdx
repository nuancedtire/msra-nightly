# Seminoma: A Common Malignancy in Young Males

Seminomas are a type of malignant germ cell tumor that originates from the epithelial cells of the seminiferous tubules in the testes. Although they account for just 1% of male malignancies, they are the most prevalent form of cancer affecting males aged 15 to 35 years. With early detection, the prognosis is favorable, showing cure rates as high as 90%.

## Risk Factors

The development of seminomas is associated with several risk factors:

- **Cryptorchidism:** Increases testicular cancer risk by 10 to 40 times.
- **Down Syndrome:** Associated with a 50-fold increase in testicular cancer risk.
- Personal or family history of testicular cancers.
- Exposure to toxic chemicals such as organophosphates and organochlorines.
- Testicular atrophy due to causes like mumps, trauma, or hormonal factors.

## Diagnosis

Seminomas typically manifest as a unilateral, painless testicular mass over several weeks or months. Some patients may seek help for infertility, which can be an associated symptom. Pain is rarely reported.

During physical examination, seminomas present as firm masses attached to the testes, occasionally accompanied by hydroceles. The only way to confirm a diagnosis of seminoma is through histopathological analysis.

## Investigations

- **Ultrasound:** The primary modality for evaluating testicular masses.
- **CT Imaging:**
  - Abdomen and pelvis CT scans aid in staging.
  - It's important to note that CT can understage up to 20% of cases.
  - Staging utilizes either the TNM system (tumor size, lymph node involvement, metastasis status) or a numerical system (Stage 1 to 3, based on the extent of metastasis).
- **Chest X-Ray:** Advised if mediastinal lymph node metastasis is suspected.
- **Serum Markers:** Beta-human chorionic gonadotropin and lactate dehydrogenase levels may indicate tumor burden. An elevated alpha-fetoprotein suggests a nonseminoma germ cell tumor.

Histology remains the cornerstone for a definitive seminoma diagnosis.

## Management

Inguinal radical orchiectomy is the standard of care for suspected seminomas, serving both therapeutic and diagnostic purposes. The management approach following surgery depends on the tumor stage:

- **Stage I, Non-Locally Invasive:** Options include surveillance, carboplatin chemotherapy, or external beam radiation therapy.
- **Stage I, Locally Invasive and Stage II:** Treatment may involve carboplatin chemotherapy, a combination of bleomycin, etoposide, and cisplatin chemotherapy, or external beam radiation therapy.
- **Stage III and IV:** Typically managed with bleomycin, etoposide, and cisplatin chemotherapy.
- For relapsed seminoma, salvage chemotherapy with mesna, vinblastine, ifosfamide, and cisplatin is recommended.
