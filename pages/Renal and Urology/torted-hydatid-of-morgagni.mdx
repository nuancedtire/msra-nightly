# Pediatric Scrotal Pain: Understanding Hydatid of Morgagni Torsion

## Introduction

The **hydatid of Morgagni** is a residual structure from the Mullerian duct, positioned at the upper pole of the testicle. Its pedunculated nature increases its risk for torsion. Remarkably, this condition is responsible for over 50% of pediatric scrotal pain cases, predominantly affecting boys aged 7 to 12.

## Diagnosis of Hydatid of Morgagni Torsion

Diagnosing torsion of the hydatid of Morgagni is mainly a clinical process. Characteristic signs include:

- Gradual onset of intense scrotal pain, often localized to the upper region of the testis.
- Swollen and tender scrotum on physical examination.
- Presence of a hard, tender nodule at the testicular superior pole, which may appear ischemic and exhibit a blue hue, known as the *blue dot sign*—though this sign appears in only about 20% of cases.

It is crucial to note that unlike testicular torsion, a torted hydatid of Morgagni typically does not result in a high-riding testicle or absence of the cremasteric reflex. Sudden onset during sleep is more indicative of testicular torsion.

## Investigations

The first-line investigation for scrotal conditions is usually color doppler ultrasonography. Although this imaging modality may not directly visualize the hydatid of Morgagni, it effectively excludes the critical differential diagnosis of testicular torsion by demonstrating normal testicular blood flow.

## Management Strategies

The torsion of the hydatid of Morgagni often resolves on its own within a week. The conservative management approach includes:

- Rest
- Application of ice
- Scrotal support and elevation
- Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
- Adequate analgesia

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** In cases where differentiation between testicular torsion and torted hydatid of Morgagni is challenging, or when the pain is severe or prolonged, surgical exploration of the scrotum is warranted.
</Callout>
