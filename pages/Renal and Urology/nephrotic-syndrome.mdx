# Understanding Nephrotic Syndrome

Nephrotic syndrome is a kidney disorder marked by a triad of symptoms including substantial protein loss in the urine, low blood protein levels, and swelling in various body parts.

## Overview

Nephrotic syndrome manifests through several key signs:

- **Heavy Proteinuria:** Urinary protein loss exceeds `>3.5g` per day.
- **Hypoalbuminemia:** Blood albumin levels drop below `<30g/L`.
- **Peripheral Edema:** Swelling is often noticeable in the lower limbs, which can also affect the abdomen or genital region.

While the majority of instances are idiopathic, some are linked to systemic conditions like:

- Type II diabetes mellitus
- Amyloidosis
- Systemic lupus erythematosus

In children, minimal change disease is the predominant cause, whereas focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) is more common in younger adults. Membranous nephropathy, often triggered by autoimmune responses against podocyte antigens, leads the list among older adults.

## Diagnosis

Patients typically exhibit:

- Swelling starting in the lower extremities
- Ascites
- Periorbital edema
- Foamy urine
- Noticeable weight gain
- Exertional dyspnea due to pleural effusion

## Investigations

Diagnosing nephrotic syndrome necessitates:

- **Proteinuria Quantification:** It can be measured via a 24-hour urine test or spot urine protein-creatinine ratio. Results of `>3.5g` per day or PCR `>300 mg/micromoles` confirm the diagnosis.

- **Urine Microscopy:** To look for cellular casts. Fatty casts indicate nephrotic syndrome, while red blood cell casts suggest nephritic syndrome.

- **Blood Tests:** These include serum albumin, urea, electrolytes, lipid levels, and liver function tests. Hypoalbuminaemia and hypercholesterolemia are common findings.

Additional tests may include:

- Serum light chain and protein electrophoresis
- HIV test
- Complement or antinuclear antibody screening

A renal biopsy is considered when diagnosis remains uncertain, especially in children unresponsive to high-dose steroid therapy, given minimal change disease is highly suspected.

## Management (Prescribing)

Treatment focuses on the root cause with immunosuppressive therapy such as corticosteroids. Alternatives include cyclophosphamide and mycophenolate mofetil. Complication prevention is crucial:

- Hypoalbuminemia
- Hypercholesterolemia
- Poor immunity
- Hypercoagulability

**Tip:** Statins are often prescribed for hypercholesterolemia due to increased atherosclerosis risk. Anticoagulants may be used for those at high risk of venous thromboembolism.
</Callout>

For mild edema, a thiazide or loop diuretic like furosemide can be beneficial.

## Management (Non-Prescribing)

Non-pharmacological strategies include:

- Elevating legs
- Compression stockings
- Dietary sodium or fluid intake restrictions
