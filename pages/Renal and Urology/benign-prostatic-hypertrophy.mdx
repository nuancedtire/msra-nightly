# Understanding Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) is a common, non-cancerous enlargement of the prostate gland, primarily affecting men as they age. This condition significantly impacts the quality of life due to its bothersome urinary symptoms. Our comprehensive guide covers the risk factors, diagnosis, investigations, and management strategies for BPH.

## Overview of BPH

BPH involves the enlargement of the prostate gland due to an increase in the number of cells (hyperplasia). It occurs in the transition zone of the prostate and is influenced by factors such as:

- **Age**: The likelihood of developing BPH increases with age.
- **Family History**: A genetic predisposition may contribute to the risk.

The condition arises from an imbalance between cell growth and cell death. Over time, enzymes like aromatase and 5-alpha reductase become more active, leading to higher levels of dihydrotestosterone (DHT) and estrogen. These hormones stimulate prostate cell proliferation.

## Diagnosis

The classical symptoms associated with BPH include:

- Nocturia (nighttime urination)
- Urinary stream weakening or terminal dribbling
- Increased urinary frequency
- Dysuria (painful urination)
- Straining during urination
- Hesitancy before urination begins
- Sensation of incomplete bladder emptying
- Acute urinary retention, sometimes accompanied by overflow incontinence
- Obstructive nephropathy (kidney damage due to urine flow obstruction)

Based on symptom severity, BPH can be categorized as mild, moderate, or severe. The combination of hyperplasia and urethral compression by the enlarged prostate leads to bladder obstruction.

## Investigations

Initial evaluations should include abdominal and digital rectal examinations, revealing a smooth, firm, and non-tender enlarged prostate. Key investigations include:

- Urinalysis to check for blood (hematuria) or infection
- Serum Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) to exclude prostate cancer
- Abdominal ultrasound, which may show:
  - Increased prostate size
  - Residual urine post-voiding
  - Hydronephrosis (kidney swelling)
  - Bladder wall thickening

For cases of chronic retention, uroflowmetry is used. A flow rate of `less than 10ml/sec` may indicate the need for surgical intervention.

## Management Strategies

### Medical Therapy

Patients with mild to moderate BPH may benefit from medication:

- **Alpha-1-Antagonists**: Drugs like doxazosin, alfuzosin, and tamsulosin relax the bladder neck and prostate muscles, aiding urine flow. Side effects can include:

  - Hypotension (low blood pressure)
  - Erectile dysfunction
  - Headaches

- **5-Alpha Reductase Inhibitors**: Finasteride and dutasteride reduce DHT production, slowing prostate growth. These medications have prolonged effects but may cause erectile dysfunction and gynecomastia (breast enlargement).

- **Parasympatholytics**: Oxybutynin may be used for patients with irritative symptoms but without increased post-voidal volume.

### Lifestyle Interventions

Modifying lifestyle factors can alleviate BPH symptoms:

- Nighttime fluid intake reduction
- Avoidance of alcohol and caffeine
- Ensuring complete bladder emptying

### Surgical Options

For those unresponsive to medical and lifestyle approaches, surgery may be considered:

- **Transurethral Resection of the Prostate (TURP)**: Preferred surgical treatment, though risks like nerve damage and impotence exist.
- **Open Prostatectomy**: Reserved for patients with significantly enlarged prostates.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** BPH can recur following TURP surgery.
</Callout>
