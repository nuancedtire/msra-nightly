# Understanding Pyelonephritis

Pyelonephritis is an inflammatory condition of the kidneys, typically caused by a bacterial infection. This article delves into the causes, risk factors, diagnosis, and management of pyelonephritis.

## Causes and Risk Factors

The kidneys may become infected through several routes:

- **Bloodstream**: Bacteria travel to the kidneys via the bloodstream.
- **Ascending Infection**: Bacteria ascend from the lower urinary tract.
- **Lymphatic System**: Rarely, bacteria spread through the lymphatic system.

The primary pathogen responsible for kidney infection is *E. coli*, but other organisms, such as *Enterococcus faecalis*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, and *Pseudomonas*, are also common, particularly with catheter use. In immunocompromised individuals, fungi and mycobacteria may cause infection. Pyelonephritis can affect anyone but is most prevalent in individuals aged 15-29.

The condition can be categorized as either uncomplicated or complicated:

- **Uncomplicated**: An otherwise healthy, non-immunocompromised patient with a normal urinary tract.
- **Complicated**: Includes all males with pyelonephritis, as the male urinary tract is inherently more complex.

### Key Risk Factors

The development of pyelonephritis can be influenced by various risk factors:

- Impeded urine flow (e.g., spinal cord injury, urinary obstruction like BPH)
- Factors promoting the retrograde ascent of organisms (e.g., short female urethra, indwelling catheter)
- Immunocompromised state (e.g., steroid use, HIV, diabetes mellitus)
- Conditions that encourage bacterial colonization (e.g., menopause, renal calculi)

## Diagnosis

Patients typically present with the following classic triad of symptoms:

- Loin pain
- Nausea and vomiting
- Fever

Additional symptoms may include those of a lower urinary tract infection and hematuria. Key differential diagnoses to consider include:

- Ruptured Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)
- Ectopic pregnancy
- Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
- Acute cholecystitis
- Renal calculi

## Investigations

Initial tests should include:

- **Urine culture**: Performed before starting antibiotics.
- **Urinalysis**: Checking for nitrites and leukocytes.
- **Pregnancy Test**: For women of childbearing age.

Hospital admission is advised for patients with signs of severe illness, dehydration, pregnancy, or if complications are present. Baseline blood tests should evaluate renal function and inflammation markers.

Imaging studies:

- **Renal Ultrasound**: To check for signs of obstruction.
- **Non-contrast CT KUB**: If an obstruction is suspected.

## Management

### Prescribing Measures

- Conduct an A-E assessment and resuscitate as needed.
- Implement Sepsis Six guidelines for severely unwell patients.

Antibiotic treatment should align with local guidelines and depend on the care setting. Common antibiotics include cephalexin, trimethoprim, and ciprofloxacin, with adjustments made once organism sensitivities are known. Supportive care includes IV fluids, analgesia, and antiemetics.

### Non-Prescribing Measures

Recurrent episodes can cause fibrosis and kidney parenchyma damage. Chronic pyelonephritis is particularly prevalent in children and those with urinary reflux from strictures.

Emphysematous pyelonephritis is a severe, gas-forming bacterial infection more common in diabetic patients. It may require interventions such as nephrostomy or percutaneous drainage, and in critical cases, nephrectomy.
