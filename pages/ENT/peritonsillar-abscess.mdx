# Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

A **peritonsillar abscess**, also known as a *quinsy*, is a collection of pus that forms outside of the capsule of the tonsil but still within the peritonsillar tissue. This condition typically arises as a complication from **acute tonsillitis** and is more prevalent in **young adults**. The infection can lead to liquefaction, walled off by fibrous tissue, resulting in pus containing primarily immune cells, and potentially some live bacteria, which makes the infection **highly contagious**. One serious risk is airway compression which can lead to obstruction.

## Causes

The most common cause of a quinsy is **Streptococcus pyogenes**. Other possible pathogens include:

- **Viridans streptococci**
- **Staphylococcus aureus**
- **Haemophilus species**

## Complications

Complications of a peritonsillar abscess may include:

- **Life-threatening airway compromise**
- Spread of infection to the **retropharyngeal space**, fascia, or **mediastinum**
- **Sepsis**
- **Aspiration pneumonia**

<details>
<summary>Common Signs and Symptoms</summary>

- **Severe throat pain** that lateralizes to one side
- **Odynophagia** (difficulty swallowing), may prevent swallowing saliva
- **Fever**
- **Dysphagia** (difficulty swallowing)
- **Referred pain** leading to otalgia (ear pain)

Other signs may include:

- **Trismus**: Reduced ability to open the jaw
- **Furred buccal mucosa**
- **Foetor** (foul breath)
- **Peritonsillar cellulitis**
- **Tender cervical lymphadenopathy**
- **Downward and medially deviated tonsil** due to quinsy, with the uvula being pushed away from the affected tonsil

</details>

## Diagnosis

Patients typically present with acute tonsillitis and subsequent worsening symptoms. Key diagnostic indicators include:

- Raised **white blood cell count**
- Increased **inflammatory markers**

In cases where pus can be drained, it should be cultured and sent for gram staining. Imaging techniques such as **X-rays** or **CT scans** may also be employed to confirm pus collections and rule out other complications.

## Management

### **Prescribing Management**

Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics are the standard treatment. Appropriate options may include:

- **Clindamycin**
- **Ampicillin-sulbactam**

### **Non-Prescribing Management**

The foremost action is to ensure the airway is **patent** and manage any compromise:

- **Drainage** of the abscess is the most common surgical intervention. This procedure typically provides immediate relief as the patient spits out the pus and some blood.
- In children, drainage is seldom necessary unless complications arise.
- In rare situations where the abscess is unresponsive to drainage or antibiotics, or in cases of severe airway obstruction, a **tonsillectomy** may be indicated.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Monitor for airway obstruction continuously, as this can progress rapidly and is a medical emergency.
</Callout>
