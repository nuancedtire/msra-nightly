# Head and Neck Cancers

Primary cancers of the head and neck region are prevalent, with **squamous cell carcinoma** being the most common type. These cancers can originate from various structures within the **oral cavity**, **oropharynx**, **larynx**, and **salivary glands**, with the lateral border of the tongue and the floor of the mouth being frequent sites for lesions.

## **Symptoms**

Symptoms can vary based on the location of the primary tumor but typically include:

- **Painful ulcerating mass** on the tongue
- **Sore throat**
- **Referred otalgia** (ear pain)
- **Bleeding**
- **Difficulty swallowing**
- **Neck lump** (from metastases to level 2 cervical lymph nodes)

## **Risk Factors**

Key risk factors for head and neck cancers include:

- **Smoking** of tobacco products
- **Alcohol** consumption
- **Betel nut chewing**
- **Chronic irritation/inflammation** (e.g., sharp teeth, ill-fitting dentures)
- **Human papillomavirus (HPV)** infection
- **Premalignant lesions** such as:
  - Oral leukoplakia (white patches of the oral mucosa)
  - Erythroplakia

> **Note:** Patients with a primary head and neck squamous cell carcinoma are at increased risk of developing secondary malignancies in the head, neck, lung, and esophagus—largely due to alcohol and tobacco exposure.

## **Diagnosis**

Diagnosis of oral cancer is confirmed through a biopsy of the lesion. Differential diagnoses for a lump or swelling in the mouth include:

### **Congenital Conditions**
- Hemangioma
- Cystic hygroma

### **Acquired Conditions**
- Ranula
- Mucous retention cyst
- Torus palatinus (osteoma of the hard palate)

### **Neoplastic Conditions**
- **Benign**
  - Salivary tumors (e.g., pleomorphic adenoma)
- **Malignant**
  - Squamous cell carcinoma
  - Salivary tumors (e.g., adenoid cystic carcinoma)
  - Sarcoma
  - Lymphoma

### **Dental Issues**
- Abscess
- Cyst

## **Investigations**

A biopsy is essential for confirming the diagnosis of oral cancer and should be performed under **general anesthesia** to obtain a sufficiently deep sample. This allows assessment of tumor invasion depth, critical for prognosis. A **pan-endoscopy** may also be conducted for thorough evaluation of the upper aerodigestive tract for synchronous second primary malignancies. Alternatively, a **positron emission tomography (PET)** or **computed tomography (CT)** scan may be utilized.

### **Staging of Cancer**

Staging is crucial for developing an appropriate treatment plan and involves assessment of:

| Evaluation Component               | Methodology                                            |
|------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| **Primary Tumor**                   | Deep perioral biopsy under general anesthesia         |
| **Regional Lymph Nodes**            | CT, MRI, or PET for spread evaluation                |
| **Distant Metastases**              | CT, MRI, or PET for spread evaluation                |

## **Management (Non-Prescribing)**

Current treatment options for oral cancer include:

- **External beam** and **interstitial radiotherapy**
- **Surgical en-bloc resection** of the tumor with reconstruction (using myocutaneous free flaps from radial forearm or fibula) for optimal cosmesis and function
- **Chemoradiotherapy**
- **Minimally invasive transoral laser resection** for large tumors

### **Treatment Preferences**

- In most early-stage cases, **primary surgery** is preferred over definitive radiation therapy due to the latter's higher morbidity risk.
- For larger and deeper tumors, **ipsilateral neck dissection** should be considered; otherwise, **adjuvant postoperative radiotherapy** is recommended.

Locally advanced oral cancers generally necessitate **multimodality treatment** due to the significant risk of locoregional recurrence and disease-related mortality. The initial approach typically involves:

1. **Surgical resection** of the primary tumor
2. **Neck dissection**
3. Followed by **adjuvant radiation therapy** or **chemoradiotherapy**

If surgical intervention is impractical, treatment options include definitive radiation therapy or chemoradiotherapy.
