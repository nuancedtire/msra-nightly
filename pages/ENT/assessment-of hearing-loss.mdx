# Assessment of Hearing Loss

## Overview of Hearing Loss Types

Hearing loss can be categorized mainly into two types: **conductive hearing loss** and **sensorineural hearing loss**. Understanding the differences between these types is crucial for effective diagnosis and treatment.

## Key Tests for Differentiation

### Rinne Test

The **Rinne test** is used to compare air conduction and bone conduction in both ears.

- **Procedure**:
  1. Place a vibrating tuning fork on the mastoid bone (bone conduction).
  2. When the sound is no longer heard, move it next to the ear (air conduction).

- **Interpretation**:
  - **Positive Rinne**:
    - Air conduction is better than bone conduction: indicates **normal hearing** or **sensorineural hearing loss**.
  - **Negative Rinne**:
    - Bone conduction is better than air conduction: indicates **conductive hearing loss**.

### Weber Test

The **Weber test** assesses lateralization of sound.

- **Procedure**:
  1. Place a vibrating tuning fork on the center of the forehead.

- **Interpretation**:
  - **Central Localization**:
    - Sound heard equally in both ears indicates **normal hearing** or **bilateral hearing loss**. 
  - **Lateralized Sound**:
    - Sound localized to one side suggests:
      - **Conductive hearing loss** on the side of lateralization.
      - **Sensorineural hearing loss** on the contralateral side.

<details>
<summary>Additional Notes on Test Interpretation</summary>

- Results from the Rinne test should always be correlated with the Weber test findings and patient symptoms.
- In cases of **conductive hearing loss**, the Rinne test will typically show negative results while the Weber test will lateralize to the affected ear.
- Conversely, with **sensorineural hearing loss**, the Rinne test will show positive results and the Weber test will lateralize to the unaffected ear.

</details>

## Important Considerations

- Be mindful of potential errors in home testing with tuning forks, as external factors may influence results.
- Future diagnostic imaging may be necessary if conductive hearing loss is confirmed; consider referring patients for audiometric testing for a comprehensive evaluation.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Ensure a thorough patient history is taken before conducting tests, as prior ear surgeries or infections may affect results.
</Callout>
