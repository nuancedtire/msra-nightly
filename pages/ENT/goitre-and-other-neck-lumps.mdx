# Branchial Cysts and Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia

## Branchial Cysts

Branchial cysts are formed due to the **incomplete closure of an embryological branchial arch**. 

### Characteristics
- **Location:** Anterior triangle of the neck, on the anterior border of the sternocleidomastoid.
- **Age of Onset:** Typically become apparent before the age of 20 years.
- **Appearance:** Smooth, slowly enlarging masses.

### Treatment
Surgical excision is the recommended treatment for branchial cysts.

<Callout emoji="⚠️">
**Warning:** Persistent or enlarging masses in this region should be evaluated to rule out other pathologies.
</Callout>

## Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN)

Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) syndromes are **autosomal dominant syndromes** characterized by hormone-producing tumors that form in multiple organs.

### MEN Type II B
- **Key Features:**
  - Medullary thyroid carcinoma
  - Phaechromocytomas (usually benign and bilateral)
  - Mucosal neuromas (bumps within the oral cavity)
  - Marfanoid appearance

### Genetic Implications
The gene involved in MEN type II B (and type II A) is the `_ret_` proto-oncogene. 

- **Testing:** Tests for `_ret_` mutations are revolutionizing MEN-2 treatment by enabling a prophylactic thyroidectomy to be performed before neoplastic changes occur, usually before `3 years of age`.

<details>
<summary>Key Considerations in MEN Management</summary>

1. **Monitoring:** Regular hormonal screening and imaging are essential for early detection of neoplastic changes.
2. **Family Screening:** Genetic counseling and testing are recommended for at-risk family members.
3. **Surgical Intervention:** Prophylactic surgeries should be considered based on individual risk factors and genetic profile.

</details>
