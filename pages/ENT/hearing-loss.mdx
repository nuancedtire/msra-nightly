# Understanding the Peripheral Hearing System

The **peripheral hearing system** is essential for sound perception and consists of three main components:

## Components of the Peripheral Hearing System

1. **Outer Ear**
   - **Pinna**: The visible part that collects sound waves.
   - **Ear Canal**: The passage that funnels sound to the tympanic membrane.
   - **Tympanic Membrane**: Also known as the eardrum, it vibrates in response to sound waves.

2. **Middle Ear**
   - An air-filled cavity containing the **ossicles**, which are:
     - **Malleus**: Connects with the tympanic membrane.
     - **Incus**: Acts as a bridge between the malleus and the stapes.
     - **Stapes**: Connects to the inner ear structures.

3. **Inner Ear**
   - **Cochlea**: Contains sensory cells crucial for hearing and is linked to the central hearing system via the **auditory nerve**. 
   - Also responsible for balance.

## Sound Transmission Process

Sound waves travel as vibrations through the air. The process of hearing involves:

- The pinna collects vibrations and funnels them into the ear canal.
- These vibrations cause the tympanic membrane to move, which transfers the vibrations through the ossicles to the cochlea.
- Hair cells within the cochlea are stimulated, determining **pitch** and **frequency** of sounds.
- This generates **nerve impulses** that travel along the auditory nerve to the brain, where sound is comprehended in the auditory cortex.

## Types of Hearing Loss

The type of hearing problem a person may experience is determined by which part of the hearing pathway is affected:

### 1. Conductive Hearing Loss
- Occurs when sound is not transferred efficiently to the cochlea, indicating that the cochlea itself is functioning.
- Common Characteristics:
  - Typically affects sound volume.
  - *Symptoms*: Difficulty hearing soft sounds (e.g., television), struggle to hear conversations.
- Examples include:
  - Blockage of wax in the ear canal.
  - Perforated eardrum.

### 2. Sensorineural Hearing Loss
- Results from problems between the cochlea and the brain, leading to abnormal sound processing.
- Causes:
  - Damage to hair cells in the cochlea.
  - Damage to the auditory nerve or defects in the auditory pathway.
- Commonly linked to:
  - **Ageing**.
  - Exposure to **excessive noise**.

### 3. Mixed Hearing Loss
- A combination of both conductive and sensorineural hearing loss.

## Summary of Hearing Mechanism

| Part of Ear | Function |
|-------------|----------|
| Outer Ear | Collects and funnels sound waves |
| Middle Ear | Transfers sound vibrations through ossicles |
| Inner Ear | Converts vibrations into nerve impulses |
