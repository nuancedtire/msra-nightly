# Understanding _Clostridium difficile_ Infection

_Clostridium difficile_ infection is a leading cause of hospital-acquired infectious diarrhea. This gram-positive, anaerobic bacterium produces toxins A and B, which can lead to pseudomembranous colitis. Transmission occurs primarily through the fecal-oral route, directly from infected individuals, carriers, or by ingesting spores.

## Diagnosis

The primary risk factor for _C. difficile_ infection is recent antibiotic use, which disrupts normal bowel flora and allows _C. difficile_ colonization. Remember the antibiotics as the 4 Cs:

- **C**ephalosporins (especially 3rd and 4th generations)
- **C**lindamycin
- **C**arbapenems
- **C**iprofloxacin, and other fluoroquinolones

Additional risk factors include:

- **Age:** Older individuals are more susceptible.
- **Healthcare Settings:** Extended hospital or nursing home stays increase risk due to potential unhygienic practices.
- **Comorbidities:** Conditions like inflammatory bowel disease heighten risk and severity.
- **Proton-Pump Inhibitors:** Although debated, they may increase risk.

### Clinical Presentation

- **Specific Symptoms:** **Diarrhea** (possibly bloody), abdominal pain.
- **Systemic Symptoms:** Fever.

Less common symptoms indicating severe colitis or complications like toxic megacolon include abdominal distension, nausea, vomiting, and shock.

## Investigations

Diagnosis and severity assessment of _C. difficile_ infection require various investigations:

- **Bedside:** Stool sample analysis using the Bristol Stool Chart.
- **Blood Tests:** Full blood count (FBC), focusing on **white cell count** (WCC) and lactate levels.
- **Imaging:** Abdominal X-ray for distension.
- **Further Tests:** 
  - CT scan for detailed abdominal evaluation.
  - Colonoscopy to confirm pseudomembranes.

## Emergency Management

Paralytic ileus, a complication of _C. difficile_, is suspected if nausea, vomiting worsen, and bowel movements cease. Imaging reveals small bowel dilation. Immediate treatment involves nasogastric (NG) tube insertion. 

Other complications such as bowel perforation and toxic megacolon require urgent intervention with antibiotics like metronidazole and/or vancomycin and possibly colectomy for severe cases.

## Medical Management

Treatment depends on infection severity:

**Mild Disease:**

- **WCC:** Normal
- **Stools:** Loose, `<3/day`
- **Treatment:** 
  1. **Oral metronidazole** 400 mg T.D.S for 10-14 days.
  2. If unresponsive or severe signs develop: **Oral vancomycin** 125 mg Q.D.S for 10-14 days.

**Moderate Disease:**

- **WCC:** Raised, `<15 x 10⁹/L`
- **Stools:** Loose, `3-5/day`
- **Treatment:** Same as mild.

**Severe Disease:**

- **WCC:** Raised, `>15 x 10⁹/L`
- **Other Indicators:** Rising serum creatinine, fever `>38.5°C`, severe colitis signs.
- **Treatment:** 
  1. **Oral vancomycin** 125 mg Q.D.S for 10-14 days.
  2. If unresponsive: **Oral fidaxomicin** 200 mg B.D, or **oral vancomycin** 125-500 mg Q.D.S ± **IV metronidazole** 500 mg T.D.S for 10 days.

**Life-Threatening Disease:**

- **Indicators:** Ileus or toxic megacolon signs, hypotension.
- **Treatment:** **Oral vancomycin** 125-500 mg Q.D.S + **IV metronidazole** 500 mg T.D.S for 10-14 days.

**Recurrent Disease (within 30 days):**

- **Positive Toxin Test:** Yes
- **Stools:** 3+/day
- **Treatment (if not life-threatening):**
  1. **Oral fidaxomicin** 200 mg B.D for 10-14 days.
  2. Alternative: **Oral vancomycin** 125 mg Q.D.S for 10-14 days.

_Antibiotics involved in the onset should be discontinued if possible. Surveillance and antimicrobial stewardship have helped reduce infection rates in the NHS. Fluid replacement is essential as in other gastroenteritis cases._

## Non-Prescribing Management

A multidisciplinary team approach is key, involving a microbiologist, infection control experts, a nurse, gastroenterologist, surgeon, and dietitian.

Public Health England's SIGHT protocol for suspected _C. difficile_ includes:

- **S**uspect infective diarrhea after excluding other causes.
- **I**solate and inform infection control.
- **G**loves and aprons.
- **H**and washing with soap.
- **T**est stool for toxin.

Surgical intervention is required for severe cases, like colectomy if caecal dilation exceeds 10 cm. Subtotal colectomy with rectal preservation is preferred.

For recurrent infections, fecal microbiota transplantation is a secondary option, restoring healthy gut bacteria.
